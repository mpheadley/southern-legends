# Anniston Star — Anniston teacher paints a new chapter with art
*By Bill Wilson, Star Staff Writer — May 9, 2025*
*Source: annistonstar.com (subscriber access)*

---

ANNISTON — After nearly three decades of shaping young minds in the Anniston City School System, third-grade teacher Michelle McGrady is preparing to retire. But for McGrady, retirement doesn't mean slowing down — it means diving headfirst into her true passion: painting.

"I'm going to devote all of my time to my art," she said, standing in the middle of her backyard studio, where color practically dances from every surface.

Walking into the 52 year old's studio is like entering a dreamscape. Colorful hand-strung beaded lamps, jars of brushes, stacks of canvases, and every hue imaginable fill the two-story space behind her home. It's a vibrant oasis nestled right in the heart of the city she loves — Anniston.

McGrady has always had a love for art, even before she began painting.

"When I would go places, I would always bring back a little original piece of art," she said. "And so I started decorating with that, and then I just poured that love into my own art."

Her journey began about 10 years ago, after both of her daughters left home. Suddenly an empty-nester, she turned her attention to painting, beginning with abstract designs on vintage doors collected from Oxford, Anniston, and even Choccolocco.

Her early pieces were bold and experimental. But it was the architectural beauty of downtown Anniston — its brick buildings, aging storefronts, and historical charm — that drew her in.

"I started noticing things that were just so beautiful," she said. "I painted the clock going straight down Noble, the courthouse, Class on Noble. I remember buying toys for my girls at Western Auto. The Downing Building — oh, I love the brick on that one."

For McGrady, painting is more than replicating structures — it's capturing memories, stories and a sense of place.

"The biggest compliment I've ever gotten," she said, "was someone telling me, 'You're not painting just buildings, you're painting part of families.' That really stayed with me."

Her art resonates because it feels personal — not only to McGrady, but to the community. Annistonians recognize their own stories in her brushstrokes. Her work now sells at Old Noble and Nunnally's Noble Frame & Gallery downtown.

One piece was commissioned by Ann Welch, a local business owner.

"I did a downtown piece for her and then some prints to go with it," McGrady said. "Now I'm looking into stationery and note cards — sets with themes like the churches or the schools."

Justin Hall, a co-owner of Old Noble said he was extremely honored when McGrady approached him about putting her artwork in the business.

"I sat down with her and we thought of ideas that would make an impact on the community to showcase her talent and came up with the hometown series," Hall said.

McGrady's range spans beyond Anniston. She's painted historic buildings in Oxford, like Hubbard's Off Main and the Oxford Cleaners. She's even ventured into Heflin and Talladega, drawn to their "rich historical heritage."

Her process is uniquely hers — wildly mixed media that includes house paint, watercolors, spray paint, acrylics, oils, and even alcohol inks. Sometimes she paints from photographs sent by others; other times, she goes to the site herself, camera in hand.

"It helps to zoom in and really see those tiny details," she said.

Despite her talent, McGrady never had formal training.

"I've just kind of been self-taught in every way, shape and form," she said. One artist who has inspired her deeply is Wyanne Thompson. "She said, 'Keep painting, your people will find you once you find your style.' I feel like this is my style now."

Her signature is just as meaningful as the work itself: "XXXOOO," a tribute to her family. The three X's and O's represent herself and her two daughters. All three have the same design tattooed on their right ankles.

"My family loves my art," she said. "One daughter is a culinary arts teacher in Pensacola. The other is in the Air Force and a prenatal nurse in Arizona. They're proud of me — and that means everything."

With retirement on the horizon, McGrady is thinking ahead. She's contemplating a series called Hometowns.

"No matter if you're from Anniston or Oxford or Mobile or Talladega," she said, "you can relate to it because it's your hometown in some way."

Some of her paintings take only a dozen hours to complete. Others stretch across days. Either way, she admits, they're rarely ever finished in her mind.

"Art is never finished, only abandoned," she said, echoing the quote often attributed to Leonardo da Vinci. "That's why, when people leave my studio, I turn the paintings face down. Otherwise, I'll just keep working on them."

McGrady's love of art is as layered as her mixed-media pieces — part memory, part tribute, all heart. And just as she's inspired countless students over the years, she's now inspiring her community — one whimsical, interpretive painting at a time.
