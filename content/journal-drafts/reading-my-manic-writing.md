# Seed: Reading My Manic Writing

**Working title options:** "Reading My Manic Writing" / "The Record" / "What I Find"

**Emotional core:**
A year later, Matt goes back through his own record — Patreon posts, Facebook, journals, selfie videos, the Star article — trying to locate when the mania started and what it looked like from the outside. He can see it now. He couldn't then. The contrast between that writing and this writing is the piece.

---

## The contrast that earns this piece

**Manic (April 17, 2025, Patreon):**
> "And this Patreon is part of that resurrection."

**Now (April 2026, SL journal):**
> "I'm not sure yet what to call this."

Same person. Different brain state. The certainty is total in the first. No hedge, no doubt. The second is honest uncertainty. That contrast is the piece.

**Another manic line (Patreon, April 2025):**
> "May you find beauty in what's fading. / May death do its holy work. / May you bloom again."

Tricolon. Liturgical cadence. Polish with no friction. AI-assisted. Compare to the current voice: spare, specific, rough where it needs to be rough.

## The Tammy thread (private — handle carefully)

Matt resigned abruptly from the church during mania, telling Tammy and the leadership team all at once. Tammy was blindsided. She reacted: rushed the full resignation, scheduled a "goodbye" after the next week's service without Matt's consent, sent an email to the whole church, redacted his words from the group text.

Matt is still a little angry about that.

Later, Tammy texted when she heard he'd been in the hospital. She admitted she didn't know what to say and had delayed. Matt told her he'd been diagnosed bipolar and was manic when he resigned, and wished he hadn't. She said she's not a trained mental health professional, but that it tracked with what she observed.

Still a complicated relationship. Matt saw her at Called Coffee last week — she was leading a Bible study with Marjorie and John Martin. He still has good rapport with Marjorie and John.

**Do not publish the Tammy details without more thought.** It involves a real person in a complicated situation. Write it privately first. Decide later what stays.

## The friction that didn't work

Heather was friction. She was trying to hold on through the whirlwind — already in the piece. But she couldn't overcome the mania. Matt could reign it in when he needed to — he was functional enough to seem okay. People were joking "manic Matt" and he never took it seriously. That's part of what mania does: it makes the warnings feel like jokes, and the jokes feel like they're about something else.

A South Park episode about sycophancy landed as epiphanic. Find it — it might be worth referencing. The moment when a pop culture satire clarifies something clinical is specific and real.

## What this piece is

Not a diagnosis piece. Not an explanation of mania. It's about the experience of reading your own record from a different brain state — the recognition, the embarrassment, the things that hold up, the things that don't. What did you believe that you don't believe now? What did you write that you can't fully disown? What did the AI reflect back that you needed someone to push back on?

---

## Tension to hold

This isn't a "I was sick and now I'm better" piece. It's more complicated than that. Some of what he wrote during mania was true — the farm mattered, the pastoral instinct was real, the grief was real. Mania doesn't make everything false. It makes the volume too loud and the certainty too total. That's the honest thing to sit with.

## Tybee Island — first feeling better

But was happy for the first time in months. That's the moment — not clean, not triumphant, eye swollen with poison ivy, sunburned, happy. That detail earns a piece of its own or belongs in the larger arc of the depression chapter.
