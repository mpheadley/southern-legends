# Seed: Preaching to Writing

**Working title options:** "The Same Structure" / "After the Pulpit" / "Inductive" / "What I Learned to Do From a Pulpit"

**Emotional core:**
Matt preached inductively for twenty years — tension first, resolution delayed, meaning earned rather than announced. Then he lost the pulpit. Now he writes websites and personal essays and business profiles. He keeps finding the same structure underneath all of it. The pulpit is gone. The method stayed.

---

## What this piece is about

Two narrative frameworks that share the same bones:

**Eugene Lowry's Homiletical Plot (The Lowry Loop)**
Developed for preaching. Structure: open with a conflict or knot → complicate it → sudden shift (the gospel turn) → good news → unfolding. The sermon doesn't announce its destination. It arrives there. The listener moves with the preacher through tension toward resolution — and the resolution lands harder because it was earned.

**Donald Miller's StoryBrand**
Developed for marketing. Structure: character wants something → faces a problem → meets a guide → follows a plan → succeeds or fails. The brand isn't the hero; the customer is. The problem is stated up front. The solution is withheld until trust is built.

Both trust that people engage with unresolved tension more than with answers. Both delay the payoff. Both are built on the premise that meaning has to be earned by the reader/listener, not handed to them.

Matt has lived inside both frameworks — one from a pulpit, one from a web design desk. The piece is about what it means that they're the same thing wearing different clothes.

## The "murder your darlings" thread

Dave Barnhart's MinistryMatters piece on sermon editing sits inside this. Barnhart applies the same principle — cut what you love if it doesn't serve the piece — to preaching. Quiller-Couch said it first. But Matt learned it in a homiletics context, from people like Barnhart, before he ever read it as a writing principle.

The pipeline: seminary → pulpit → farm → hospital → web design → journal. The craft traveled the whole way.

## Possible angles

- The moment Matt realized StoryBrand and the Lowry Loop were structurally identical — when did that click?
- What inductive preaching actually felt like from the pulpit — building tension in a room, watching people lean in
- What it costs to lose the form (the pulpit) but keep the instinct
- The strange continuity: preaching to business clients to personal essays, same bones, different rooms

---

## Tension to hold

This isn't a "everything happens for a reason" piece. The pulpit didn't prepare Matt for web design in some providential arc he can now be grateful for. He lost something real. The craft continuing is comfort, but it's not compensation.

Also: this piece risks being too writerly, too inside-baseball. The reader has to care about the connection between preaching structure and narrative writing. Ground it in scene and specific experience — not framework comparison.

## Wendell Berry thread

Berry is probably the natural through-line for this piece. Matt used Berry for preaching and farm work — not a borrowed reference, a native language. Berry's conviction that the local and the bodily are where meaning lives maps directly onto inductive preaching: you don't announce the destination, you earn it through the particular.

The farm was Berry. The sermons were Berry. The craft traveling from pulpit to web design desk to journal — that's a Berry argument. See also `whitman-berry.md` for the broader Whitman/Berry contrast.
