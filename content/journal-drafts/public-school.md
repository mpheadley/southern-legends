# Seed: Public School

**Working title options:** "Public School" / "New Friends" / "What the Move Cost"

**Emotional core:**
The kids were homeschooled until the farm sold. Then they moved to Pleasant Valley. Then public school — new friends, new routines, a life that wouldn't have existed without the collapse. Matt drives them to a friend's house and passes farms on the way. The pang is in the context of that: the kids are okay, maybe more than okay, and the farm is still gone.

The hidden cost of the collapse wasn't just Matt's — it was the kids' whole world. Homeschool, the farm, the land, the life they knew. All of it ended when it ended. And then something new started that they didn't choose either.

---

## What this piece is about

The disruption the kids absorbed without being asked. The way collapse lands differently on children — they don't process it the same way adults do, they just adjust, and sometimes the adjustment looks fine, and that's almost harder to hold.

Not a piece about guilt. More like: the farm ending had a shape that extended past Matt and Heather. The kids are in Pleasant Valley now, making friends, going to public school. That's real. And the old life is still gone.

---

## Specific details to hold

- Homeschooled until the move — the farm was the context for that life
- New public school in Pleasant Valley after the sale
- Driving to a friend's house — a friend they wouldn't have had otherwise
- Passing farms on the way
- The pang in that context: life continuing, loss still present

---

## Tension to hold

This isn't a "silver lining" piece. The new friends are real, but they exist because something was taken. Both things are true and neither cancels the other. Don't resolve it.

Also: the kids' experience is theirs, not Matt's to narrate fully. Write from his vantage point — what he observes, what he carries — not from inside theirs.

---

## Constraint: Heather's read before publishing

The kids are in this. Their transition, their loss, their new life. Heather reads it before it goes anywhere. This is a family piece, not a solo decision.

Also: no identifying details about the kids — no names, no ages, no photos. Matt's voice, Matt's observation, but the kids stay protected.

---

## When to write it

When there's more distance from the transition. This is still recent — Pleasant Valley, new school, new friends. Write it when it's had time to settle and the observation doesn't feel like it's still happening.
