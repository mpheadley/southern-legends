# Writing Authentically: A Guide for Southern Legends

A practical guide for writing long-form editorial profiles that sound like a human being wrote them — because a human being did.

---

## Part 1: What Makes Writing Sound Human vs. AI-Generated

The core difference between human and AI writing is not quality — it is texture. Human writing has grain, knots, and irregularities. AI writing is sanded smooth.

### Signals Readers Trust (Human Tells)

**Concrete specificity.** Human writers name the actual thing. Not "a Southern restaurant" but "the Waffle House on 431 where the waitress calls everyone 'baby.'" Specificity is the strongest human signal because AI models, trained on aggregate patterns, default to the general. ([Source: AI Writing vs Human Writing](https://hastewire.com/blog/ai-writing-vs-human-writing-examples-key-differences))

**Temporal and spatial anchoring.** Humans write "last Tuesday" or "the summer my dad sold the truck." AI writes "in recent years" or "throughout history." Real memory has coordinates.

**Imperfection as evidence of life.** Minor grammatical quirks, sentence fragments, colloquialisms, regional phrasings — these are not errors. They are proof someone was actually there. AI text is almost never ungrammatical, and that perfection itself becomes a tell. ([Source: Signs of AI-Generated Text](https://detector-checker.ai/blog/signs-of-ai-generated-text-vs-human-written-text/))

**Vulnerability without performance.** Human writers share things that cost them something. Not "I struggled with doubt" (AI can write that) but the specific moment, the specific room, the thing you almost said but didn't. AI mimics sentiment but rarely conveys raw vulnerability — its emotional passages come off as cliched or hollow. ([Source: AI vs Human Writing Differences](https://hastewire.com/blog/ai-writing-vs-human-writing-examples-key-differences))

**Unexpected connections.** Humans make associative leaps — connecting a fishing trip to grief, a biscuit recipe to class anxiety. AI connects things that are obviously related. Humans connect things that shouldn't be related but somehow are.

**Anecdotal openings.** AI-generated essays tend to get straight to the point without anecdotal introductions. Human writers often start with a scene, a moment, a story — because that is how memory works. ([Source: Distinguishing AI from Human Writing](https://www.insidehighered.com/opinion/career-advice/teaching/2024/07/02/ways-distinguish-ai-composed-essays-human-composed-ones))

### Signals Readers Distrust (AI Tells)

**Uniform sentence rhythm.** AI paragraphs often contain sentences of nearly the same length, creating a steady, metronome-like pace. Human writing naturally varies — short punches followed by longer unwinding sentences, fragments next to compound-complex structures. ([Source: Common AI Sentence Structures](https://michellekassorla.substack.com/p/recognizing-ai-structures-in-writing))

**Formulaic scaffolding.** AI loves the "From X to Y" construction, the "Whether...or..." opening, the present participial phrase tacked onto the end of a sentence ("creating a sense of warmth"). These are templates, not thoughts.

**The consensus middle.** AI gravitates toward language that is statistically probable — the most likely next word. This produces text that is correct but never surprising. It reads like a well-researched committee report, not a person thinking on the page.

**Symmetrical structure.** AI paragraphs often mirror each other: same number of sentences, similar openings, balanced transitions. Human writing is asymmetrical because human thought is asymmetrical.

**Abstract over concrete.** AI defaults to abstractions ("a sense of community," "the challenges of modern life") where a human writer would give you the thing itself.

## Part 2: Techniques from Literary Journalism and Narrative Nonfiction

The writers you admire — Wright Thompson, John Jeremiah Sullivan, Margaret Renkl, the Bitter Southerner crew — share certain craft moves. These are not tricks. They are disciplines.

### Wright Thompson: The Space Between Stories

Thompson's central insight for profile writing: **"If you're going to write profiles, I find it's most interesting to live in the space between the stories they tell about themselves and the stories others tell about them. Figure out where that disconnect is to tell something that is true."** ([Source: Indiana Media School](https://mediaschool.indiana.edu/news-events/news/item.html?n=espns-wright-thompson-find-your-passion-and-treat-it-like-a-craft))

His craft principles:

- **The subject is never just the subject.** His Michael Jordan piece is really about aging and identity loss: "I wanted to write about what do you do when you used to be Michael Jordan." The profile of a person is really about a universal human question. "The things that last are the stories that are about all of us."
- **Observe deeply.** Develop curiosity about details others walk past. The telling detail is never the obvious one.
- **Cut through myth and brand.** Thompson says the profile writer's job is like a lie detector — getting past the story someone has rehearsed to find the one they haven't told yet.
- **Master fundamentals before breaking rules.** "Learn the rules before you start breaking them." Build skill through smaller stories before attempting complex narratives.
- **No shortcuts.** "There is no magic trick" — only outworking the material through consistent effort over years. ([Source: Indiana Media School](https://mediaschool.indiana.edu/news-events/news/item.html?n=espns-wright-thompson-find-your-passion-and-treat-it-like-a-craft))

### John Jeremiah Sullivan: The First Person Earned

Sullivan's key moves from his [LA Review of Books interview](https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/pulp-nonfiction-an-interview-with-john-jeremiah-sullivan/):

- **Start in dialogue with something.** "My pieces almost always begin with me in dialogue with something." He never begins from nothing — something has already provoked him. A book, an image, a conversation, a place. The essay is a response.
- **Deploy autobiography strategically.** Sullivan doesn't include himself in every piece, and when he does, it is because his personal experience provides genuine access to the subject. His evangelical high school phase gave him real entry into his Christian rock essay. His rural Indiana background gave him access to Axl Rose. The first person must "take me somewhere the piece couldn't go" — it earns its place.
- **Tame the first person.** He compares learning to use "I" in nonfiction to hearing your voice on tape — you must learn to hear it the way a reader hears it. This takes years.
- **Read voraciously.** Sullivan says reading is "indispensable" — his brain works through dialogue with texts, so more exposure means more creative connections firing.
- **Trust serendipity, but work for it.** "If you keep hurling yourself at the wall, eventually a crack will open." What looks like luck in reporting is really persistence.

### Margaret Renkl: Poetry Disguised as Prose

From her [Southern Review of Books interview](https://southernreviewofbooks.com/2021/10/13/graceland-at-last-margaret-renkl-interview/):

- **Listen to the music of language.** Renkl's poetry background means she reads every essay aloud, removing words and sentences that interrupt the flow. Language must "sound like [the words] belong to one another."
- **Trust the story to do the work.** Rather than argue a position directly, she uses narrative. She references parables — "trust the story to do the work of conversion." She writes about politically charged topics "in a way that is as apolitical as possible" by making them personal.
- **Write to discover.** "Sometimes I don't know what I think till I see what I've written." The draft is where the thinking happens — not before it.
- **Love revision.** Renkl genuinely prefers revising to first-drafting. She treats revision like "kneading bread" — pulling, lifting, folding. She steps away overnight when possible.
- **Keep it brief and vivid.** Her essays are notably short — micro-essays that compel the reader to sit with each sentence. Density over length.

### Core Craft Moves Across All These Writers

1. **Scene over summary.** Build scenes with characters, dialogue, setting. Show people in action rather than telling the reader about them. This is the fundamental unit of narrative nonfiction. ([Source: National Centre for Writing](https://nationalcentreforwriting.org.uk/writing-hub/unlocking-the-art-of-creative-non-fiction-tips-for-writing-compelling-narrative-stories/))

2. **Sensory immersion.** Engage multiple senses — not just sight, but sound, smell, texture, taste. The reader should forget they are reading.

3. **Dialogue as character.** In creative nonfiction, people talk to one another. Dialogue reveals character more efficiently than description. Let people speak in their own idiom.

4. **Delay the thesis.** Literary journalism does not announce its point in the first paragraph. It earns its meaning through accumulation — scene, detail, scene, reflection, and then the reader arrives at the meaning alongside the writer.

5. **Structural surprise.** The best narrative nonfiction is not organized by topic but by movement — emotional, temporal, associative. The structure itself is part of the argument.

6. **The telling detail.** One right detail does the work of a paragraph of description. The chipped coffee mug, the way someone fidgets with a ring, the specific hymn playing on the radio.

## Part 3: Southern Editorial Voice Without Cliche

### The Bitter Southerner Principles

Chuck Reece founded The Bitter Southerner in 2013 specifically to destroy the two dominant media stereotypes of the South: "dumb hillbillies" or "Gone With the Wind types." The truth, he says, is that the South is "a wonderfully diverse place filled with smart and beautiful people doing amazing things." ([Source: NPR Code Switch](https://www.npr.org/sections/codeswitch/2015/07/25/425315875/q-a-with-chuck-reece-a-bitter-southerner-reimagines-the-region))

Their editorial philosophy:

- **Tell true stories of the South as it is** — "in all its beauty and brokenness." Not one or the other. Both, held together.
- **Never gloss over the bad parts.** "You can't just write anything about Southern history that glosses over the bad parts." Reece uses gumbo as a metaphor: okra was brought by enslaved Africans, so you cannot celebrate the dish without acknowledging that history.
- **Reject the monolithic South.** There is no single "Southern experience." There are as many ways to be Southern as there are Southerners. ([Source: The Bitter Southerner / Josina Guess](https://bittersoutherner.com/from-the-southern-perspective/how-to-write-a-better-south-josina-guess))
- **Find the great stories in the middle** — between the genteel myth and the redneck caricature. That middle is where most actual Southerners live.
- **Amplify community voices.** Their Folklore Project and guest editor series let diverse perspectives emerge organically rather than filtering everything through a single editorial lens.

### How to Write Southern Without Performing Southern

**Don't explain the South to outsiders.** Write for people who live here. When you write for an imagined Northern reader, you start translating — and translation flattens everything. The Bitter Southerner succeeds because it writes *from* the South, not *about* the South for someone else.

**Avoid the accent on the page.** Phonetic spelling of dialect ("Ah reckon") is almost always patronizing. Let word choice, syntax, and rhythm carry the voice instead. A Southerner's sentence structure is already distinct — you don't need to misspell words to prove it.

**Resist the nostalgia trap.** Nostalgia without honesty is propaganda. You can love a place and acknowledge its failures. Margaret Renkl does this in every essay — writing about the beauty of the natural South while confronting its political and racial realities. The Bitter Southerner's editorial mandate is to "remember and hold on to all the good that's worth keeping while tenaciously exposing and getting rid of the junk."

**Be specific about place.** Not "the South" — NE Alabama. Not "a small town" — Fort Payne, Mentone, Guntersville. Place names carry weight. They ground the reader and resist generalization.

**Let people be complicated.** The best Southern writing treats its subjects as full human beings — not symbols of Southern resilience, not colorful characters, not avatars of decline. Real people contain contradictions. Leave the contradictions in.

**Don't perform warmth.** Southern hospitality is real, but on the page it can become a tic. Not every essay needs a front-porch metaphor. Trust that the warmth comes through in how you pay attention to people, not in how many times you mention sweet tea.

**Earn your right to the hard subjects.** Race, poverty, religion, the Confederacy — these are not decoration or backdrop. If you are going to write about them, do the reporting. Talk to people. Read the history. Bring the same rigor you would bring to any serious subject.

## Part 4: Using AI as a Writing Collaborator Without Losing Your Voice

Research from a [2024 academic study on creative writers and AI](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.03137v2) found that writers who maintain their voice treat AI as a specific-purpose tool, not a co-author. Here is what works.

### When to Write from Scratch (Always)

- **Personal essays and reflections.** The process of writing these *is* the content. AI cannot access your memory.
- **Scene-setting and sensory description.** If you were not there, neither was the AI. Your observations are the material.
- **Voice-dependent passages.** The opening, the emotional turns, the ending — these must be yours.
- **Any passage where vulnerability matters.** If it doesn't cost you something to write it, it will not give the reader anything.

### Where AI Can Help (With Guardrails)

- **Research and fact-gathering.** Background on a place, a historical event, a person's public record.
- **Structural feedback.** "Here is my draft. Does the structure work? Where does the energy drop?" Use AI as a reader, not a writer.
- **Brainstorming angles.** "I'm writing about a blacksmith in Fort Payne. What are ten unexpected angles I might explore?" Then throw away seven of them and use the three that spark something real.
- **Editing for clarity.** "Here is a paragraph. Is anything unclear? Are there unnecessary words?" But make the actual cuts yourself.
- **Transcription and organization.** Turning interview notes into organized material. But the shaping is yours.

### Prompting Strategies That Protect Your Voice

**Feed it your writing first.** Before asking AI to help with anything, give it several paragraphs of your own writing and say: "This is my voice. Match this tone, rhythm, and level of specificity. Do not use words I would not use."

**Be directive, not open-ended.** Not: "Write me an introduction to this profile." Instead: "I'm opening with the scene at the workshop. Draft three possible first sentences that start with a concrete sensory detail — something I might see or hear walking in the door. Keep them under fifteen words."

**Reject and revise.** The [academic study](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.03137v2) found that writers who maintain ownership "have read every single word and put their stamp of approval on it." If AI generates something usable, rewrite it in your own syntax before keeping it. Change the word order. Replace the adjectives. Make it yours.

**Use AI for the parts you hate.** If you hate writing meta descriptions or social media copy, let AI draft those. Save your creative energy for the actual writing.

**Never let AI write your endings.** The ending is where meaning lives. If an AI writes your ending, it is not your essay.

### The Two-Draft Rule

1. **Draft one: all you.** Write the full piece yourself, ugly and imperfect. Get the scenes down, the quotes, the observations, the emotional arc. Do not touch AI during this phase.
2. **Draft two: AI as editor.** Give your draft to AI and ask specific questions: Where is this unclear? Which paragraph drags? Is there a better order for these three scenes? Then make the changes yourself, by hand.

This preserves the most important thing: the draft that comes from your own thinking, your own memories, your own attention. AI polishes. You create.

## Part 5: AI Writing Patterns to Actively Avoid

### The Kill List: Words and Phrases

These words and phrases appear dramatically more often in AI-generated text than in human writing. Some are fine in moderation — the problem is frequency and clustering. If you see several of these in one piece, something has gone wrong.

**Verbs that signal AI:**
delve, foster, leverage, harness, underscore, highlight, showcase, navigate, bolster, streamline, optimize, unlock, unleash, elevate, align, enhance, empower, revolutionize, supercharge, transform

**Adjectives and adverbs:**
robust, comprehensive, pivotal, crucial, vital, seamless, dynamic, nuanced, multifaceted, innovative, cutting-edge, unprecedented, vibrant, intricate, fundamentally, notably

**Phrases that scream machine:**
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..." (107x more common in AI text — [GPTZero](https://gptzero.me/news/most-common-ai-vocabulary/))
- "This plays a significant role in shaping..." (182x more common in AI text)
- "At the end of the day..."
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's explore"
- "The intersection of X and Y"
- "A tapestry of..."
- "It is important to note..."
- "From X to Y, ..."
- "Whether you're a...or a..."
- "In an era of..."
- "This is not just about X — it's about Y"
- "Moreover" / "Furthermore" / "Additionally" (as paragraph openers)
- "In conclusion"
- "Aims to explore"
- "Notable works include" (120x more common in AI — [GPTZero](https://gptzero.me/news/most-common-ai-vocabulary/))

([Source: Plus AI](https://plusai.com/blog/the-most-overused-chatgpt-words), [Blake Stockton](https://www.blakestockton.com/red-flag-words/), [GPTZero](https://gptzero.me/news/most-common-ai-vocabulary/), [Embryo](https://embryo.com/blog/list-words-ai-overuses/))

### Structural Patterns to Break

**The tricolon reflex.** AI loves groups of three: "creativity, innovation, and determination." Humans sometimes list two things. Sometimes four. Sometimes one, with a comma and a pause.

**The mirror paragraph.** AI paragraphs often follow the same internal structure: topic sentence, explanation, example, concluding thought. Vary your paragraph shapes. Some paragraphs should be one sentence. Some should be eight.

**The hedging reflex.** "It could be argued that..." / "One might say..." / "In many ways..." These are the verbal equivalent of backing slowly out of a room. If you believe something, say it. If you don't, don't write it.

**The false balance.** AI defaults to presenting "both sides" even when one side is obviously right. This is not nuance — it is cowardice. Real nuance means holding complexity, not splitting the difference.

**Emoji-style emotional signaling.** AI sometimes writes emotional content that reads like stage directions: "This was a deeply moving experience." Instead of labeling the emotion, recreate the conditions that produced it. Let the reader feel it themselves.

**The summary conclusion.** AI almost always ends by restating what it just said. Human essays end with an image, a scene, a question, a line of dialogue — something that opens outward rather than closing down.

### The Rhythm Test

Read your piece aloud. If every sentence lands with the same weight, at the same pace, something is wrong. Human writing breathes. It speeds up in excitement. It slows down in grief. It stops short when something catches it off guard.

A passage that sounds human:

> He opened the shop at six every morning for forty-one years. Forty-one years. Same key, same lock, same fluorescent flicker when the lights came on. I asked him if he ever got tired of it. He looked at me like I'd asked if he got tired of breathing.

A passage that sounds like AI:

> For over four decades, he demonstrated an unwavering commitment to his craft, opening his shop each morning with a dedication that spoke to his deep passion for the work. His consistency showcased a remarkable resilience that inspired those around him.

The difference: the first passage has a specific detail (the fluorescent flicker), repetition that earns its keep (forty-one years, said twice), sentence length variation (from twelve words to four), and an ending that shows character through reaction rather than telling you what to feel. The second passage tells you things ("unwavering commitment," "deep passion," "remarkable resilience") without showing you anything at all.

## Headley Web Blog — Specific Notes

The blog posts on headleyweb.com are primarily SEO vehicles. Nobody's browsing the blog for the prose — they're landing from a Google search, scanning for the answer, and maybe clicking through to services. That changes the calculus, but not in the way you'd expect.

**What "SEO-first" means for voice:**
- **Shorter is better.** 400–500 words, not 1000. Get in, be real, get out.
- **The opening matters more, not less.** If someone lands here, the first two sentences decide if they trust you. A generic opening loses them. A real one hooks them.
- **The AI kill list still applies.** Google's helpful content update downgrades content that reads like it was written for search engines instead of people. A post that sounds human performs better in search, even if nobody reads every word.
- **Voice still differentiates.** If a potential client clicks three blog posts and they all sound like every other web design blog, you're invisible. If one of them sounds like a real person in Jacksonville who talked to a woodworker at a kid's birthday party — that sticks.

**The bar is lower on craft but not on authenticity.** Keep it short, keep it real, still apply the guide.

**TLDRs — keep them on Headley Web, skip them on Southern Legends:**
- Headley Web blog posts are SEO-first. Google's AI Overviews and featured snippets pull from concise summary text — a TLDR in frontmatter serves that purpose. It may also feed meta descriptions or structured data depending on how the blog template renders it. Respects the reader who's scanning for an answer.
- Southern Legends profiles are editorial — meant to be read, not scanned. A TLDR would undercut the narrative, like putting a spoiler at the top of a short story.

## Summary: The Rules

1. **Be specific.** Name the road, the song, the brand of coffee. Specificity is the single strongest signal that a human being was paying attention.
2. **Vary your rhythm.** Short sentences. Then a longer one that winds around and takes its time getting where it's going. Then short again. Like breathing.
3. **Earn the first person.** Use "I" only when your presence takes the story somewhere it couldn't otherwise go.
4. **Show, then trust.** Build the scene. Give the reader the details. Then stop. Don't explain what it means. They'll get there.
5. **Write the first draft yourself.** Always. Every time. The first draft is where the thinking happens.
6. **Revise by reading aloud.** If it sounds like a press release, cut it. If it sounds like you talking to someone you trust, keep it.
7. **Resist smoothness.** A piece that is too polished, too balanced, too orderly is a piece that has had the life edited out of it.
8. **Write from the South, not about it.** You live here. Write like you live here.
9. **Let people be people.** Not symbols, not characters, not representatives of anything. People.
10. **When in doubt, add a detail and remove an adjective.**
