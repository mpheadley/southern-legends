I grew up seeing covered bridges in movies and calendars and assuming they were a New England thing. Turns out I was wrong. Alabama has some of the finest covered bridges in the country, and most of them are within an hour of where I live.

The oldest one in the state is eight miles from my front door.

## Coldwater Covered Bridge — Oxford, Alabama

The Coldwater Covered Bridge sits in Oxford Lake Park, about four miles south of Anniston. It was built around 1850 — some sources say as early as 1839 — by a formerly enslaved man, possibly a forerunner of Horace King, the freed slave who would become one of the most celebrated bridge builders in the Deep South.

That fact alone is worth pausing on. The oldest covered bridge in Alabama was designed and built by someone who was not free. The craftsmanship — a 63-foot span using a rare combination of Multiple King-post truss and Town Lattice — has outlasted almost everything else from that era. The bridge was originally located over Coldwater Creek on what's now Airport Road, near the old Hughes Mill. It partially burned in 1920, survived, carried motor vehicle traffic until 1974, and was moved to Oxford Lake Park in 1990 where the City of Oxford maintains it today.

It's been on the National Register of Historic Places since 1973. And it's right here in Calhoun County — the kind of thing you drive past without knowing what you're looking at.

## The Blount County Covered Bridge Trail

About an hour west of Anniston, Blount County holds three covered bridges that you can visit in a single afternoon. They've earned the county the unofficial title of "Covered Bridge Capital of Alabama," and honestly, it's not a stretch.

### Horton Mill Bridge (1934)

Horton Mill is the one that stops people cold. At 70 feet above the Calvert Prong of the Little Warrior River, it's the highest covered bridge above any waterway in the United States. Not Alabama — the entire country.

The original bridge was built in 1894, but the current structure dates to 1934, when a 15-man crew led by Talmedge Horton — a descendant of the family the bridge is named for — spent a year and a half building the 220-foot Town Lattice truss span. It was the first covered bridge in the southeastern United States added to the National Register of Historic Places, in 1970.

The bridge was closed to motor vehicles in 2022 due to safety concerns, but it's still open to pedestrians. Walk to the middle and look down. Seventy feet to the river. It's something.

<PullQuote>At 70 feet above the water, Horton Mill is the highest covered bridge in the United States.</PullQuote>

### Swann Bridge (1933)

The Swann Covered Bridge — also called the Joy Covered Bridge — stretches 324 feet across the Locust Fork of the Black Warrior River. It became the longest covered bridge in Alabama after the 385-foot Nectar Bridge burned in 1993. Three spans, Town Lattice truss, wood and metal combination construction. It was listed on the National Register in 1981, closed for repairs, and reopened to motor vehicles in 2012.

Driving across Swann Bridge is the experience that makes people fall in love with covered bridges. The light changes as you enter. The boards thrum under your tires. And 324 feet later, you come out the other side wondering why we ever stopped building things like this.

### Easley Bridge (1927)

Easley is the small one — 95 feet across Dub Branch, built by a crew led by foreman Forrest Tidwell and his nephew Zelma. It looks even smaller than its measurements suggest, tucked into the trees like it's been there since the land was settled. Which, at nearly a hundred years old, it practically has been.

Easley is currently the only covered bridge in Blount County you can still drive across. That makes it both the most modest and the most functional of the three. Sometimes the small ones are the ones that last.

## Why They Matter

Covered bridges were practical structures — the roof protected the wooden trusses from weather, extending the bridge's life by decades. But somewhere along the way, they became more than that. They became landmarks, meeting points, the kind of places people drive out of their way to see and photograph.

In a region where a lot of historic structures have been torn down, burned, or left to rot, these bridges survived because people cared enough to maintain them. Blount County installed cameras at all three bridges in 2015 after vandals tagged the Easley Bridge with graffiti. The communities that host these bridges take ownership of them — not because there's money in it, but because some things are worth keeping.

The Coldwater Bridge in Oxford was moved eight miles to save it. Horton Mill was closed to cars rather than risk collapse. Swann was repaired and reopened. Easley still carries traffic at nearly a hundred years old. Every one of these bridges exists today because someone decided it was worth the trouble.

If you've never done the Blount County Covered Bridge Trail, it's an easy afternoon trip from anywhere in Calhoun County. And if you haven't walked through the Coldwater Bridge at Oxford Lake Park, you've been overlooking the oldest covered bridge in Alabama right in your own backyard.

*The Coldwater Covered Bridge is located at Oxford Lake Park, off State Route 21, Oxford, AL.
