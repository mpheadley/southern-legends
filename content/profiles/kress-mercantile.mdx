The S.H. Kress Company built this building in 1935 with the intention of it being a "jewel" on Noble Street. They succeeded — then time did what time does to jewels that nobody polishes. For decades, the building sat there accumulating drop ceilings, worn tile, and the kind of slow deterioration that makes people walk past a historic structure without realizing what's underneath.

Then Andy Koehler and Toby Kilpatrick bought it and started ripping things out.

## The Building

What they found under the renovations was worth the gamble. Eighteen-foot ceilings. Original Art Deco moldings — every inch of them intact. Dark, rich wood floors that had been hiding under layers of flooring nobody asked for. The bones of a building that was designed to be beautiful and had simply been waiting for someone to let it be beautiful again.

Koehler and Kilpatrick didn't hesitate. They rewired the building, tore out everything that wasn't original, and restored the interior to something close to what the Kress Company had envisioned ninety years ago — while adding modern utilities that make the space actually usable. The building is listed on the National Register of Historic Places as a contributing property in the Downtown Anniston Historic District. That designation isn't honorary. It means the building matters, architecturally and historically, and these two decided to act like it.

## Two Lives

The Kress has had two lives under its current owners. The first was as an event venue — the Kress Grand Ballroom — offering one of the most beautiful rental spaces in the region for weddings, corporate events, and community gatherings. The restored interior, with those ceilings and moldings, gives the space a grandeur that most event venues in this part of Alabama can't touch.

The second life is the Kress Mercantile, which opened its doors in July 2024. Koehler and Kilpatrick transitioned the ground floor into a curated marketplace featuring over a dozen local vendors. Handcrafted clothing, unique gifts, home goods, locally sourced honey — the kind of carefully selected inventory that makes a shop worth visiting rather than just passing through.

The dual model works because the building supports it. The event space draws people for special occasions. The Mercantile gives them a reason to come back on a Tuesday. Both uses keep the building active, which is ultimately what historic preservation is about — not freezing a structure in amber, but finding a use that justifies its continued existence.

## Toby Kilpatrick's Bet

Toby Kilpatrick is a Birmingham native and longtime Atlanta resident who moved to Anniston because he saw potential. That's a sentence that means more than it sounds. People don't move to small Alabama cities from Atlanta on a whim. Kilpatrick is a real estate developer who looked at downtown Anniston, saw what was possible, and put his money where his conviction was.

He's been vocal about believing that downtown Anniston's revival is real and growing. The Kress is his most visible proof point, but the thesis is bigger than one building: that a walkable, historic downtown with local businesses, good architecture, and community investment can compete for attention and dollars, even in a region that's been losing both for decades.

I build websites for local businesses. I spend a lot of time thinking about what makes a place worth visiting, worth finding online, worth driving to. The Kress Mercantile is the kind of anchor that changes the equation for an entire block. When someone walks into that building and sees those ceilings for the first time, the question shifts from "is downtown Anniston worth visiting?" to "what else is down here?"
