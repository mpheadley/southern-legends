Most people experience Weaver, Alabama, at 45 miles per hour. It sits between Anniston and Jacksonville on the road that connects the two, and unless you have a reason to stop, you probably don't. The town doesn't announce itself. There's no grand entrance, no welcome center, no scenic overlook. It's just a small town doing small-town things, and from the highway, that's all you see.

I had a reason to stop. For seven years — 2014 to 2021 — I pastored Weaver First United Methodist Church. And in that time, Weaver taught me more about what community actually means than any seminary class ever did.

## The Town

Weaver is a city of about 3,300 people in Calhoun County. It was settled in the 1830s after the forced removal of the Cherokee, and the town takes its name from brothers Simeon and Linzey Weaver, early settlers whose family put down roots here before the Civil War. The first school — Cave Creek Academy — went up in 1861. By 1868, the railroad came through. David Weaver, son of Linzey, donated land for a post office, a depot, and served as both postmaster and station agent. The town almost got renamed — Weaverdale and Weaverburg were floated in the 1890s — but Weaver stuck.

The town didn't incorporate until 1945, when citizens voted 60 to 4 in favor of a formal government. Fort McClellan, just up the road, drove population growth and shaped the town's identity for decades. When the fort closed, Weaver adapted the way small towns do — quietly, without fanfare, by holding onto what worked and letting go of what didn't.

## What I Found There

When I arrived at Weaver First UMC in 2014, I was coming from a church in Muncie, Indiana. I didn't know Alabama. I didn't know Calhoun County. I definitely didn't know Weaver. What I found was a small church — a few dozen people on a Sunday morning — with an outsized commitment to the community around it.

The church runs a community garden. It partners with Community Enabler, Interfaith Ministries, Boy and Girl Scouts. It has a basketball gym and — this still makes me smile — a roller skating rink. The church's self-description is "a small church with a big vision: growing a garden of disciples through transformational worship, radical hospitality, and extravagant service." I helped write some version of that language, and I can tell you it wasn't aspirational. It was descriptive. The people in that church meant it.

Seven years of Wednesday prayer hours and Sunday mornings. Seven years of hospital visits, funeral planning, potluck dinners, and the kind of slow, accumulating trust that you can't rush. I performed weddings for people's children. I sat with families in the worst moments of their lives. I watched a congregation that could've folded — small membership, limited budget, aging building — choose instead to keep showing up. Not because it was easy, but because that's what you do.

## What Small Towns Actually Are

There's a tendency to romanticize small-town life or to dismiss it. Neither one is right. Weaver isn't a postcard. It has the same problems every small town in Alabama has — limited resources, aging infrastructure, young people leaving for bigger cities. The school debate that comes up every few years. The businesses that open and close. The road that needs paving.

But Weaver has something that's harder to quantify: a civic stubbornness that refuses to let the town become less than what it is. The city park has a BMX track, a skate park, basketball and tennis courts, a walking trail, and picnic facilities — in a town of 3,300. The Chief Ladiga Trail passes right through. Jacksonville State University is six miles away. The town isn't isolated. It's connected, modest, and deliberate about what it offers.

## Why I'm Writing This

I left Weaver First UMC in 2021 to take a position at Anniston First United Methodist. That chapter of my life is over now too, for reasons I've written about elsewhere on this site. But Weaver is the place that shaped me as a pastor, and the people there are the reason I know what community means when it's not just a word on a church sign.

When I think about what Local Legends is — why I write these stories, why I care about the people and places in this part of Alabama — a lot of it traces back to a small church on Anniston Street in a town most people drive through without looking up. Weaver showed me that the best work happens in the places nobody's watching. And the people who do it don't need recognition. They just need someone to notice.

I noticed.
