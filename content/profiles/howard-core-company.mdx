I have a music degree. I spent years studying voice at Jacksonville State University, performing in operas and musicals, surrounded by musicians who took their instruments as seriously as their own breathing. And I had no idea — none — that one of the largest violin dealers in America was operating out of Anniston, Alabama, fifteen minutes from where I went to school.

Howard Core Company. 99 Symphony Way, Anniston. Over 1,800 violin shops, music stores, and luthiers across North America buy their instruments, bows, cases, strings, and tools from this address. If you've ever walked into a violin shop anywhere in this country and picked up an instrument, there's a decent chance it passed through Anniston on its way to you.

## The Story

Howard and Myrna Core started the company in New York in 1976, dealing in the violin family of stringed instruments — violins, violas, cellos, basses, and everything that goes with them. In late 1977, they moved the operation to Alabama. The reasons for that move are the kind of practical calculation that drives a lot of business relocations: lower costs, more space, a central location for shipping. But the result was something nobody planned — a world-class instrument distribution company rooted in a town most violinists have never heard of.

The company deals exclusively in stringed instruments, which is a narrower focus than most people realize. Their 400-plus page catalog is essentially an encyclopedia of everything a luthier or violin shop might need: instruments from makers and manufacturers around the world, bows at every price point, cases, strings, rosin, tools, books, replacement parts, repair supplies. Their house brands — including Johannes Köhr — have built a reputation for honest value at the student and intermediate level.

In 2001, Alex Weidner joined Howard Core as a partner, forming Howard Core Company LLC. The partnership was designed to ensure the company's future beyond its founders, and the operation has continued to grow. Forty employees work out of the Anniston facility, all focused on the same thing: getting the right instrument into the right player's hands through the shop owners and luthiers who serve them.

## Why It's Remarkable

The United States has plenty of instrument dealers. What it doesn't have are many that operate at this scale from a city of 22,000 in northeast Alabama. The address — 99 Symphony Way — is almost too perfect. It sounds like it belongs in Vienna or New York, not off a side road in Calhoun County. But that's where it is, and that's where it's been for nearly fifty years.

I think about this in the context of everything else I write about on this site. Anniston has a museum that rivals big-city institutions. A national monument commemorating a defining moment in civil rights history. Mountain bike trails that draw athletes from 34 states. And a violin company that supplies half the string shops in North America. At some point, the pattern stops being a collection of surprises and starts being a statement about the kind of place this is — a place where serious people do serious work, regardless of whether the rest of the world is paying attention.

## For Musicians

If you're a string player, a teacher, or a luthier, you probably already know Howard Core — even if you didn't know the company was based in Alabama. If you're not in that world, here's what you should know: the craft of stringed instruments is alive and well, it depends on a supply chain that most people never see, and a significant piece of that supply chain runs through Anniston.

As someone who studied music at JSU and spent years in rooms full of musicians, I wish I'd known about Howard Core when I was a student. Not because I play strings — I'm a vocalist — but because understanding that this kind of company exists here would have changed how I thought about what's possible in this part of the state. You don't have to leave to do world-class work. Sometimes world-class work is already happening on a road called Symphony Way, and all you have to do is notice.
