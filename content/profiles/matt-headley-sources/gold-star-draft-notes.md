## The Scene

Dr. Hadley's office. Large room. Books on the sofa. Big desk with a computer she types on rapidly — the keyboard clicking while she occasionally glances up to show she's listening. She does this often. Matt has been with her two years.

He told her he'd published an article in the Anniston Star about his depression and recovery. They talked about how the mental health experience could be a powerful aspect of his ministry. He was doing better. The visit was wrapping up.

She handed him a gold star sticker. He put it on his shirt — he thinks.

---

## What Was Actually Happening

Neither of them knew it at the time.

She suspected he might be a little manic — sleeping very little. She gave him a prescription for sleep meds. He was downplaying the sleep issue. He thought the prescription was optional. He never had it filled.

She didn't diagnose him bipolar that visit. She wouldn't have given him the sticker if she had known. She suspected. He didn't.

He walked out with a gold star on his shirt and an unfilled prescription in his pocket.

---

## The Turn

The essay hinges on this: at the time of the gold star, both of them thought the hard chapter was over. It wasn't. The Anniston Star article had a tidy ending. The recovery wasn't complete. The mania was already there, unrecognized.

## The Anniston Star Article (October 21, 2024)

Full text saved in `anniston-star-hope-in-wilderness.md`.

It ends: *"Resurrection isn't just a thing of the past. It's what happens when love brings us back to life."*

The byline identifies him as "associate pastor and music director at Anniston First United Methodist Church." He is no longer either of those things.

The article is entirely first-person recovery narrative — tidy, resolved, pastoral. Written while he was almost certainly already manic. He describes "ego death" and a new lightness and freedom as part of his healing. That was the mania.

He never mentions sleeping very little. He never mentions the prescription.

The article came out in the Anniston Star on October 21, 2024. I ended it like this:

*"Resurrection isn't just a thing of the past. It's what happens when love brings us back to life."*

I meant it.

Dr. Hadley's office is large. Books on the sofa. A big desk with a computer she types on rapidly — I remember the sound of the keyboard clicking. She would occasionally glance at me to show she was listening. She does this often. I've been with her two years.

I told her about the article. We talked about how the mental health experience could be a powerful aspect of my ministry — having been through depression and come out the other side. I was doing better. [That's what I told her. I believed it.]

As the visit wrapped up, she congratulated me and gave me a gold star sticker. She handed it to me. I put it on my shirt, I think.

She also gave me a prescription for sleep meds. I was sleeping very little. She told me later — after she diagnosed me bipolar — that she thought I might have been a little manic at that visit. She suspected. She didn't know yet. She wouldn't have given me the sticker if she had known.

I was downplaying the sleep.

I kind of thought the prescription was optional.

I never had it filled.
