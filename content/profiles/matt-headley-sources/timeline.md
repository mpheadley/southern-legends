## The Full Arc (Chronological)

### Chapter 1: The Life That Made Sense (2006–2024)
- 19 years of ministry — music director, pastor, worship leader
- Flower farm launched 2021 with Heather in Anniston (Lenlock Heights neighborhood, behind Walmart), **1832 farmhouse**
- Regenerative farming: cut flowers, workshops, markets, wedding flowers, **Bloom Bar** (market flower sales), **Trail Porch** retail kiosk on Chief Ladiga Trail
- Permaculture mentors: **Trevor and Joanna Mann of WaldenFARMacy** — animal husbandry, agroforestry, regenerative ecosystem design
- Community engagement: Shepherd's Table breakfast ministry, interfaith work, trail development committee, grant writing
- Digital content: YouTube vlogs ("Start Seeds With Me"), online worship during pandemic
- Family performing together: A Christmas Story: The Musical (Gadsden, 2024)
- Acclaimed Christmas services at Anniston First (Lessons & Carols, O Magnum Mysterium, 2023–2024)

### Chapter 2: The First Wilderness — Clinical Depression (Summer 2024)
- Severe clinical depression hit around turning 40
- **At this point, diagnosed as depression — NOT bipolar.** The bipolar diagnosis came later.
- Wrote about it publicly in **The Anniston Star** (Oct 21, 2024): "Hope in the Wilderness: A Pastor's Journey Through Depression"
- Key themes from that article (Matt's own words):
  - "Severe depression feels like being lost in total darkness"
  - Hiking in the woods as literal wilderness during depression
  - Sitting by Camp Lee pool surrounded by people, feeling completely disconnected
  - Wrestling with God — journaling anger, grief, frustration
  - Letting go of bias against psychiatric medication: "Yes, my serotonin is store-bought!"
  - "Ego death" — the part that craved control and perfection relaxed
  - Called it **resurrection** — "not just of my spirit, but my whole self"
  - Heather's patient love pulled him through
  - Shepherd's Table ministry — realizing he wasn't so different from the people he served
  - Psalm 139: "Where can I go from your Spirit?"
- **Recovered.** Went back to ministry. Thought he was through it.

### Chapter 3: The Manic Episode → Bipolar Diagnosis (late 2024 / early 2025)
- **Not yet written about publicly.** This is the first telling.
- A manic episode followed the recovery — public, devastating
- **The manic episode is what led to the bipolar diagnosis.** The first depression had been treated as just depression. The mania revealed it was bipolar disorder all along.
- **While manic, Matt quit his job at the church** — not a deliberate decision, a symptom
- **While manic, Matt overextended the family financially on the flower farm business**
- These two actions — quitting and overspending — are what caused the collapse. The mania drove both.
- Lost the ministry, the financial stability, credibility, relationships, daily rhythm
- The Foothills article (May 2025) frames it carefully: Heather says "Matt went through a mental health crisis last year" and that he "left his position at the church to focus on farming full time"
- **The second destruction was more utter and complete than the first.** The depression took him to the edge; the mania burned everything down.
- **Public narrative in spring 2025:** The Patreon post (April 17, 2025) frames the departure as a deliberate, sacred choice: "I stepped away from my role as a local church pastor to become a full-time flower farmer." This was the story at the time — he hadn't yet shared (or fully understood) the bipolar framing publicly.

### Chapter 3.5: Trying to Make It Work (April–July 2025)
- After leaving the church, Matt and Heather went all-in on the farm business
- **Patreon launched** (April 17, 2025) — "mobile tabernacle," sharing farm life, spiritual reflections, book project
- **Foothills Magazine** profile published (May 7, 2025) — public-facing optimism about the farm
- **Guest preaching:** Father's Day / Trinity Sunday (~June 15, 2025) at **Jacksonville First Presbyterian Church** — sermon titled "When Wisdom Played" (livestreamed, starts at 15:45). Significant: invited back to a pulpit within 3 months of leaving ministry.
- **New business offerings (July 2025):** à la carte wedding flowers, resin jewelry from pressed flowers (on website), photo shoot at Spring Street Station with Jessica Trammell Photography
- **Bloom Bar** at markets, **Trail Porch** kiosk active
- **Behind the Blooms** video series on Patreon (S1 E2, July 15, 2025)
- **Patreon tiers:** $5/month (greeting card), $15/month (coloring book PDF — new), $25/month (resin jewelry)
- **Patreon stats (as of bio page):** 27 members, 5 paid, $26.44/month
- **Additional Anniston Star articles** published spring/summer 2025 (referenced in July 1 Patreon: "pieces published the past couple months") — details/titles TBD
- **Financial reality underneath:** Prepaid team through fall via PayPal credit line. "Current on bills, but it's tight — every dollar is weighted with intention." Anxiety about money, Heather's health, slow sales.
- **Key metaphor emerging:** "The pulpit isn't gone. It's just mobile now." — still preaching through content, conversation, rebuilding. "A shepherd with a shovel instead of a stole."
- **Scripture anchoring him:** Romans 5:3–5 (CEB) — trouble → endurance → character → hope

### Chapter 4: Depression Again, Hospitalization & Selling the Farm (November 2025)
- After months of trying to make the farm work, **bipolar depression hit again** — this time with the full weight of seeing what the manic episode had cost
- **Hospitalized for bipolar depression** — November 2025
- **Farm sale closed November 2025** — they no longer have it
- **Relocated to Pleasant Valley, AL** (Jacksonville mailing address) — also November 2025. Return to Heather's roots.
- All three events — hospitalization, farm sale, relocation — happened simultaneously
- **Matt has been embarrassed to come out of hiding.** The awareness of what happened during the manic episode — the choices that weren't really choices — has made it hard to face people.
- This has NOT been written about publicly yet

### Chapter 5: Getting Stability (late 2025–present)
- Now in **Pleasant Valley, AL** (Jacksonville, AL mailing address)
- Heather grew up in Pleasant Valley — this is a return to her roots
- **Getting stability again** — medication, new diagnosis, new understanding of what he's dealing with
- **Headley Web & SEO is in early launch stage** — actively reaching out for referrals, looking for first clients. Hormozi flywheel strategy: free/discounted builds → portfolio + testimonials → raise prices. See `BUSINESS_STRATEGY.md` and `PLAN.md` for details. **For the profile, keep business details minimal — just honest about being in the early days.**
- Created **Local Legends** — editorial storytelling site about NE Alabama people and places
- Four kids still all hands on deck
- **Heather and the family never gave up on him.** The destruction felt complete for a long time — but it wasn't, because they were still there. Former vocations (ministry, farm) are over for now, but the family held.

## Voice & Tone Notes
- Matt's Anniston Star article is the best reference for his authentic writing voice
- The Patreon posts add another dimension: more informal, more financially transparent, more community-facing
- Spiritual but not preachy — uses "resurrection" and "grace" naturally, not performatively
- Self-deprecating humor: Mario Kart reference, "store-bought serotonin," "permaculture unicorn," hashtags like #weknowthis and #nailinthecoffin
- Vulnerable but not pitying — states hard facts plainly, then moves forward
- Uses concrete sensory details: Camp Lee pool, hiking in the woods, handing someone flowers, drying books on the trampoline, Sunny barking in the background
- Theological depth worn lightly: Maundy Thursday, Trinity Sunday, mobile tabernacle, Romans 5 — integrated naturally, never lecture-y
- The Local Legends profile should be MORE vulnerable than the Star article (that was written mid-recovery; this is written from the other side of a second collapse)
- **The Patreon posts reveal a gap between the public narrative (spring/summer 2025) and the private reality.** In April he was projecting hope and sacred purpose. By July he was still farming, still preaching, still building. By November it all collapsed. That gap — the months of trying to make it work — is part of the story.
