For nineteen years, my life made sense. I served as a pastor and church music director, building worship services and leading congregations through the kinds of moments that shape a community: weddings, funerals, Wednesday nights, Sunday mornings. And for the last four of those years, my wife Heather and I homesteaded out of an 1832 farmhouse in Anniston, Alabama — she grew the flowers while I helped where I could, and together we raised sheep, chickens, vegetable gardens, and four kids. We sold bouquets from our Bloom Bar at local markets and from a little kiosk we called the Trail Porch, right on the Chief Ladiga Trail.

I loved the work. Not in a résumé way — in the way that you love something that becomes the architecture of your days. The creativity of leading a worship service or turning a compost pile. The slow trust that builds between a pastor and a congregation. The satisfaction of harvesting something you put in the ground months ago and handing it to somebody who lights up when they see it.

Then it all came apart. Twice.

## The First Wilderness

The summer I turned forty, severe depression pulled me under. The clinical kind: the kind where you sit by the pool surrounded by people and you can't understand why you can't just be happy. Like watching yourself from above with frustration and disappointment and fear. I hiked alone in the woods a lot that year. I wrestled with God in my journal. I let go of my long-held bias against psychiatric medication. I wrote about all of it in *The Anniston Star*, because I thought if I was honest about what the wilderness felt like, maybe somebody else in the dark would feel less alone.

And it worked — on me, at least. Something in me relaxed. The part that needed to control everything and look fine and earn my place. I called it an ego death. I called it resurrection. I went back to the pulpit. I thought I was through it.

I wasn't through it.

## The Second Destruction

What I didn't know yet was that the depression was only half the diagnosis. Within months, a manic episode followed — public, devastating, and nothing I would have chosen. It revealed what had been underneath all along: bipolar disorder. The depression had been the first act. The mania was the second, and it burned everything down.

<PullQuote>You don't lose one thing. You lose the version of yourself that held everything together.</PullQuote>

I lost the ministry. I lost the farm. I lost relationships, credibility, and the daily rhythm that had defined my adult life. Not all at once — there were months where I tried to hold it together, selling flowers, guest-preaching for a friend who pastors nearby, launching a Patreon, writing for the paper. I told myself the pulpit wasn't gone, it was just mobile now. And maybe that was true. But the ground was already shifting.

By fall, the farm was sold. The life Heather and I had built in that old farmhouse — the animals, her flower rows, the Trail Porch — all of it, gone. We moved to Pleasant Valley, back to the place where Heather grew up. Starting over in the most literal sense.

There's no softening that. It happened. And for a while, the only honest answer to "what do you do?" was "I'm figuring that out."

## Building in Public

What I had, underneath the rubble, was a set of skills I'd been using for years without thinking of them as a career. I'd built websites for my church and our flower farm. I understood how to make something clear, functional, and honest — how to take what a person or organization actually does and put it in front of the people who need to find it. I just hadn't called it a profession yet.

So I started [Headley Web & SEO](https://headleyweb.com). Not with a grand launch or a marketing budget — with the kind of stubborn, one-foot-in-front-of-the-other energy that comes from having no other option. I build websites for local service businesses: plumbers, contractors, HVAC companies, the people whose work keeps Northeast Alabama running but whose online presence usually doesn't do them justice.

<PullQuote>Starting over doesn't mean starting from nothing. It means finding out what you had all along that you never thought to count.</PullQuote>

I'm good at it. Not because I went to school for it — because nineteen years of ministry taught me how to listen to what people actually need, and four years of homesteading taught me that nothing grows without patience and consistent work. The technical skills are real, but the instinct for people is what makes the sites work.

## Why Local Legends Exists

Then I built this site.

Local Legends started as a portfolio project — a way to show potential clients what I could do with editorial design and storytelling. But it became something else. It became the thing that made the rebuilding feel like more than just survival.

The idea is simple: Northeast Alabama is full of people and places whose stories don't get told. Not because they aren't worth telling, but because nobody's writing them down. The outreach center that feeds hundreds of families. The museum that rivals institutions in cities ten times Anniston's size. The nonprofits running on volunteer hours and stubbornness. I know what it feels like to be invisible — to have your story reduced to the worst chapter. So I write the stories of places and people who deserve to be seen for what they actually are.

It's not charity. It's not therapy, though it probably helps with that too. It's community building — the same work I did as a pastor, just in a different form. Instead of a pulpit, it's a website. Instead of a congregation, it's a town. The impulse is the same: find the people doing good work, and make sure somebody notices.

## What Comes Next

I'm not going to pretend the hard part is over. Bipolar disorder doesn't go away because you found a new career. Rebuilding a reputation in a small town takes longer than building one in the first place. There are still days when the grocery store feels like a gauntlet.

But I'm building. The web business is growing. Local Legends is publishing real stories about real places. Heather and the kids are still here — they never weren't. And every profile I write is a small act of defiance against the idea that your worst moment gets to define you — mine included.

If you know me, you probably know some version of this story already. This is the version I'd like you to hear: not the fall, but what I built after it. And if you know someone in Northeast Alabama whose story deserves to be told, I'd love to hear about them.
