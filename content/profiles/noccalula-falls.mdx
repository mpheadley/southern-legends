The first time I stood at the edge of Noccalula Falls, I understood why people have been coming here for two hundred years. Ninety feet of water dropping straight off a ledge into Black Creek gorge, mist rising, the sound filling the ravine until it's the only thing you hear. It doesn't need a brochure. It doesn't need me to write about it. But I'm going to anyway, because most people outside Etowah County have no idea it's here.

## The Falls

Noccalula Falls is a 90-foot waterfall in a 250-acre park on the edge of Gadsden. The water drops from Black Creek into a ravine that you can hike down into, walk behind, and explore for hours. The gorge is lined with caves, rock overhangs, and the kind of geology that makes you realize the Appalachian foothills have been doing their work for a very long time.

The park's been here since 1950, when the City of Gadsden finally purchased the land after decades of trying. The property changed hands several times before that — Thomas McClung claimed the falls and 40 acres around them in 1845. A man named Faxon carved his name into the rock wall behind the waterfall in 1859, and it's still there. In the second half of the 1800s, someone operated a tavern and dance hall in a cave behind the falls, then tried to expand the flat space inside with dynamite. It collapsed. Some ideas are better on paper.

## The Legend

The name Noccalula likely comes from the Cherokee term *ama uqwalelvyi* — roughly, "place where water thunders." But the story that stuck is the one about a Cherokee girl who threw herself off the falls rather than marry someone her father had chosen instead of her true love. It's the kind of legend that dates to the late 1700s and may have roots in real events at the Cherokee village of Turkey Town, near present-day Gadsden.

In 1969, the Woman's Club of Gadsden commissioned Belgian sculptor Suzanne Silvercruys to create a statue. The result is a nine-foot, 3,000-pound bronze figure of Noccalula, caught in the act of leaping, placed at the edge of the waterfall itself. It's dramatic, a little unsettling, and impossible to forget once you've seen it.

Whether the legend is history or myth, it's become inseparable from the place. The falls are named for her. The statue watches over them. And every visitor who walks up to the overlook gets the story, whether they came for it or not.

## The Gorge

The falls are the centerpiece, but the gorge is where you lose track of time. The Black Creek Trail is a 1.7-mile crushed stone path that winds through the ravine past caves, an aboriginal fort site, an abandoned dam, pioneer homestead, and Civil War-era carvings scratched into the rock walls by soldiers who passed through here 160 years ago.

It's a strange mix of natural beauty and layered history — Cherokee, Civil War, early settlers, Depression-era civic ambition — all compressed into a single gorge in a single park. You can hike through it in an hour, but you'll want longer. There's always something carved into a wall or tucked into a cave opening that you didn't notice the first time.

The park also has botanical gardens, a sightseeing train, playground, picnic pavilions, campground, and a wedding chapel — which is the kind of list that sounds like it's trying too hard, but somehow the park pulls it off. The falls anchor everything. As long as 90 feet of water is dropping into a gorge, the rest is just extra.

## Going

Noccalula Falls Park is one of those places I send people to when they ask what there is to do around here. It works for families with kids, for hikers, for history people, for anyone who just wants to stand next to something genuinely impressive and not have to drive three states to find it. It's right here, on the edge of Gadsden, and it's been right here since long before any of us showed up.
