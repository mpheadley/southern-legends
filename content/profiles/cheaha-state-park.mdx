People are always surprised that Alabama has mountains. Not big mountains — we're not Colorado — but real mountains, with elevation and rock faces and views that stretch across ridgeline after ridgeline until the haze swallows everything. Cheaha Mountain is the highest point in the state at 2,407 feet, and the state park that wraps around it is one of the best things Alabama ever built.

I say "built" deliberately. The Civilian Conservation Corps got here in 1933 and spent six years constructing Bunker Tower, stone cabins, a lake, and hiking trails — all by hand, all from local stone, all still standing. Cheaha State Park is Alabama's oldest continuously operating state park, and the CCC work gives it a weight that newer parks can't fake. You can feel the craftsmanship in the stonework. Ninety years later, it holds up.

## The Summit

Here's the thing about Cheaha that surprises highpointers: it's easy. You can drive to the top, park next to Bunker Tower, and climb a short set of stairs to stand at 2,407 feet. No scramble, no permit, no four-hour approach hike. It's one of the most accessible state high points in the country, and the view from the tower is worth the drive by itself — layered ridgelines of the Talladega National Forest fading into distance in every direction.

But if you want to earn it, the trails are there.

## The Trails

The park sits at the Cheaha Trailhead of the Pinhoti Trail, which winds through the Talladega National Forest and eventually connects to the Appalachian Trail in Georgia. That alone makes it significant — this is where the Southeast's long-distance trail network touches Alabama.

For day hikes, the options range from easy to genuinely challenging:

**Bald Rock Trail** is a 0.3-mile walk to an overlook at the mountain's edge, with a wheelchair-accessible boardwalk section. It's the fastest way to a big view.

**Pulpit Rock Trail** is another short one — steeper terrain, rocky outcropping at the end, and one of the best sunset spots in the state.

**Cave Creek Trail** drops 3.3 miles into the Cheaha Wilderness with serious elevation change and views that reward the effort. It's the trail that reminds you this is real Appalachian terrain, not a gentle park loop.

**Mountain Laurel Trail** follows a stream through laurel thickets to Rock Garden Outlook — quieter, less trafficked, and beautiful in spring when the laurel blooms.

## The Drive

The Talladega Scenic Drive — Highway 281 — is the way in, and it's half the experience. The road winds along the ridgeline through Talladega National Forest with the kind of views that make you pull over repeatedly. In fall, the color is serious. Not Vermont serious, but serious enough that you'll stop pretending Alabama doesn't have seasons.

The park is roughly halfway between Birmingham and Atlanta, which puts it within day-trip range of a lot of people who've never heard of it. That's the recurring theme of this whole region — world-class natural attractions hiding in the part of the state most people drive through on their way somewhere else.

## Why I Keep Going Back

I live in Pleasant Valley — just outside Jacksonville — about 40 minutes from the park entrance. Cheaha is my default answer when someone asks for a weekend recommendation. Families, hikers, photographers, couples who want a cabin with a view — it works for all of them. The stone cabins and chalets have real character, the restaurant sits at the top of the mountain, and the campground is one of the better ones in the state park system.

But mostly I go back for the quiet. On a weekday morning, you can stand on Pulpit Rock and hear nothing but wind. You're at the highest point in Alabama, looking out at the southern Appalachian foothills, and for a few minutes nothing else exists. That's hard to find, and it's 40 minutes from my house.
