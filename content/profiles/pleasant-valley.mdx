Pleasant Valley, Alabama, is not a place you find on purpose. It's not on the way to anywhere. There's no exit on the interstate, no historic marker, no reason for a stranger to turn down the road unless they're lost or looking for someone who lives here. It's a community in Calhoun County, near Jacksonville, that exists because the people who live here decided it should — and that's been enough to keep it going.

My wife Heather grew up in Pleasant Valley. When everything else in our lives fell apart — the ministry, the farm, the life we'd built in Anniston — this is where we came back to. Not because we chose it from a list of options. Because it was the place that was still standing when nothing else was.

## What's Here

I'm not going to pretend Pleasant Valley has a vibrant downtown or a bustling local economy. It doesn't. What it has is an elementary school, a high school with a 95% graduation rate, churches, families who've been here for generations, and the kind of silence that either drives you crazy or puts you back together. Depending on the week, I've experienced both.

The community sits near the Calhoun-Cleburne county line, at the edge of where the Appalachian foothills start to rise in earnest. Cheaha State Park — Alabama's highest point — is a short drive east. The Talladega National Forest is right there. Jacksonville and JSU are close enough to keep you connected. But Pleasant Valley itself is quiet. Deliberately, stubbornly quiet.

The schools matter here. Pleasant Valley High School is ranked in the top 65 high schools in Alabama, which is the kind of fact that surprises people who've never heard of the place. The community pours into those schools the way small towns do — because the schools are the center of gravity. Football games, graduation ceremonies, the annual rhythms that mark the year.

## Why I'm Writing About It

Every other profile on this site is about a place or an organization with a public identity — a museum, a trail, a nonprofit, a historic bar. Pleasant Valley doesn't have that. It doesn't have a website or a visitors bureau or a story that makes for an easy headline. It's just a place where people live.

I'm writing about it because it's home now, and because the story of this place — for me — is inseparable from the story of starting over. When Heather and I moved here in late 2025, we were coming off the worst year of our lives. I'd been hospitalized. We'd sold the farm. The career I'd built over nineteen years was gone. We had four kids, a diagnosis I was still learning to live with, and the kind of humility that only comes from watching everything you thought defined you disappear.

Pleasant Valley didn't ask us to explain any of that. It just had room.

## The Quiet Places

I've learned something in the past year about the places that don't make lists. The places with no sign out front and nothing to prove. They hold people. Not in a dramatic way — not with programs or services or strategic plans — but in the way that land holds a house. By being there. By being solid. By not requiring anything from you except that you show up and keep going.

Heather's roots here go back decades. Her family is here. The land is familiar. And for someone rebuilding a life — which is what I'm doing, one website and one story at a time — there's a value in familiar ground that I can't overstate. You don't always need new. Sometimes you need the place that already knows you, or at least knows the person you came from.

Pleasant Valley won't make the tourism brochures. It won't draw weekend visitors or generate search traffic. But for me, it's the answer to a question I didn't know how to ask: where do you go when you need to start again?

You go home. Or if you're lucky, home finds you.
