Every Sunday morning at 8:30 — before the choir warms up, before the ushers straighten the bulletins, before anyone in the congregation settles into a pew — Anniston First United Methodist Church opens its doors for Shepherd's Table. It's a breakfast ministry. Free meal, no questions asked, open to anyone who walks in. Most of the people who show up are unhoused. Some are just hungry. Nobody has to explain which one they are.

I served at Shepherd's Table during one of the hardest seasons of my life, and it rearranged something inside me that needed rearranging.

## What It Is

The mechanics are simple. Volunteers from the church and the broader community arrive early on Sunday mornings to cook and serve breakfast. The meal is ready by 8:30. People come in, sit down, eat. There's conversation, warmth, and the kind of dignity that comes from being fed at a table rather than handed a bag.

Shepherd's Table operates on the same campus where Interfaith Ministries runs its programs, where the United Way Warming Station opens during cold weather, where The Bridge hosts community events. Gurnee Avenue on a Sunday morning is a place where the church's stated mission — "a community transformed" — is either real or it isn't. Shepherd's Table is one of the reasons it's real.

There's no application process. No intake form. No eligibility screening. You're hungry, you eat. That simplicity is deliberate and, in my experience, rare. A lot of ministries and nonprofits that serve people in crisis build in layers of process that exist to protect the organization more than they help the person. Shepherd's Table doesn't do that. It just feeds people.

## What It Taught Me

In the summer of 2024, I was in the middle of severe clinical depression. The kind where you sit by the pool surrounded by people and you can't understand why you can't just be happy. The kind where you hike alone in the woods because the silence makes more sense than conversation. I've written about this publicly — in The Anniston Star, on this site — so I'm not going to rehash the full story here.

What I will say is this: serving at Shepherd's Table during that season changed my relationship to ministry. Not because I had some dramatic epiphany. Because I looked across a table at someone eating breakfast — someone whose life was harder than mine by any measure — and realized I wasn't there to save them. I was there because we were both showing up to the same place, at the same time, needing something we couldn't name.

The part of me that needed to be the pastor, the leader, the one with answers — that part got very quiet at Shepherd's Table. What replaced it was something simpler and more honest: I'm a person who can scramble eggs and pour coffee and sit with another person for twenty minutes on a Sunday morning. That's enough. That's the whole ministry.

<PullQuote>I realized I wasn't there to save them. We were both showing up to the same place, needing something we couldn't name.</PullQuote>

## The Volunteers

Shepherd's Table runs because people show up. Not a paid staff. Not a program director with a budget and a strategic plan. Volunteers from the congregation who set their alarms early on a day most people sleep in, because they've decided that this is how they want to start their week.

I've been in ministry long enough to know that the programs people volunteer for on Sunday mornings — the ones that cost them comfort and sleep — are the ones they believe in most. Nobody shows up to Shepherd's Table for the résumé line. They show up because they've sat across from someone who's sleeping outside and decided that person deserves a hot breakfast and a conversation.

That sounds small. It's not.

## The Bigger Picture

Anniston First UMC's campus on Gurnee Avenue has become something of a social services hub in downtown Anniston — not by grand design, but by accumulation. Shepherd's Table on Sunday mornings. Interfaith Ministries providing emergency assistance during the week. The Warming Station at The Bridge during cold weather. Community events, conferences, shared volunteers moving between church work and service work.

The campus is proof that a church can be more than a building where people worship for an hour on Sundays. It can be a place where the community's needs are met, consistently, by people who've decided that showing up is not optional.

I'm no longer on staff at First UMC. But Shepherd's Table is still there, every Sunday, 8:30 a.m. The eggs are still getting scrambled. The coffee is still getting poured. And someone who spent the night outside is sitting at a table being treated like a person, which is the most basic and most radical thing a church can do.

<PullQuote>Someone who spent the night outside is sitting at a table being treated like a person.</PullQuote>
