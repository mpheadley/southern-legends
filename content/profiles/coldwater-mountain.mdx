I'll be honest — when I first heard people talking about Anniston as a mountain biking destination, I thought they were being generous. This is a town that most Alabamians associate with the Army Depot, the old Fort McClellan, maybe the museums if they've been paying attention. Mountain biking capital of anything? That's a stretch.

Then I rode Coldwater Mountain, and I stopped being skeptical.

## The Numbers

Over 56 miles of singletrack on 4,083 acres of Alabama Forever Wild land, tucked between Anniston and Oxford. More than $4 million invested in trail development since 2012. An IMBA Bronze Ride Center designation, with Silver in the works. And in October 2025, Life Time — the fitness brand behind the Leadville Race Series — chose Coldwater for the first Leadville Trail 100 qualifying event east of the Mississippi.

Over 600 athletes from 34 states and Canada showed up for that inaugural race. Life Time spent four years scouting locations in the Southeast before picking Anniston. That's not hometown boosterism. That's an outside organization with no reason to be polite telling you this place is legit.

## The Trails

The trail system has something for everyone, but it's the intermediate and advanced riding that sets Coldwater apart. Bomb Dog is the signature trail — incredible flow, elevation changes, and views that remind you this is the Appalachian foothills, not some flat state park somewhere. Gazza is on the other end of the spectrum: rocky, technical, the kind of descent that earns its reputation.

The newest addition is Southern Traverse, an 8.5-mile intermediate connector that spans the length of the property from one end to the other. It links the trail network together in a way that lets you string together rides of almost any length without repeating yourself.

Two trailheads provide access: the Coldwater Trailhead at 1661 Coldwater Road and the Anniston Trailhead at 401 Monsanto Street. Parking is open sunrise to about an hour or two after sunset.

## How This Happened

The story of Coldwater Mountain is really a story about what happens when the right people decide to push. In 2012, IMBA Trail Solutions drew up a master plan for 60 miles of trails on the Forever Wild tract. The Northeast Alabama Bicycle Association (NEABA) has been the driving force on the ground — organizing volunteers, advocating for investment, and doing the unglamorous work of trail maintenance that keeps a system like this rideable.

Tom Nelson, former NEABA president, has been vocal about the $4 million-plus that's gone into the mountain. More recently, Innovate Alabama invested $1.1 million in trail improvements. The state declared 2025 "The Year of Alabama Trails," and Coldwater was a centerpiece of that campaign.

None of this was inevitable. There's no reason a mid-sized Alabama city should have a world-class mountain biking destination. But someone had a vision, other people invested in it, and the trails got built. That's how it works.

## What It Means for Anniston

I build websites for local businesses. I think about economic development and search traffic and what brings people to a place. And I can tell you that Coldwater Mountain is the most significant tourism asset Anniston has added in decades.

Mountain bikers travel. They book hotels, eat at restaurants, buy gear, fill up gas tanks. When 600 athletes show up from 34 states for a single weekend event, that's money flowing into a local economy that could use it. When someone in Atlanta or Nashville searches "best mountain biking in Alabama," Anniston shows up now. That wasn't true ten years ago.

The trail system also does something less measurable but equally important: it gives people a reason to feel good about where they live. I've talked to locals who had no idea this was here until someone dragged them out for a ride. Same reaction every time — surprise, then pride. *This is in Anniston?*

## Still Growing

Coldwater Mountain isn't done. The push for IMBA Silver status would put Anniston in an even smaller group of recognized mountain biking destinations. The Coldwater Trail Race is expected to return, with NEABA taking over ownership of the event going forward. And there's still room to build on the original 60-mile master plan.

For now, the trails are open, the parking lot is free, and the riding is as good as anything in the Southeast. If you've been sleeping on Coldwater Mountain, stop. Grab a bike, pick a trailhead, and go find out what everyone's been talking about.
