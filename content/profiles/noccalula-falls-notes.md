# Noccalula Falls — Fact-Check Notes

Verified 2026-03-26.

---

## Turkey Town location
**Claim in article:** "may have roots in real events at the Cherokee village of Turkey Town, near present-day Centre, Alabama"
**Verdict:** Corrected. Original draft said "near present-day Gadsden" — changed to Centre.

Dominant historical record (Wikipedia, Encyclopedia of Alabama, majority of 38 historical maps examined by historian Amos J. Wright Jr.) places Turkeytown near Centre, Cherokee County — about 30 miles from Gadsden. The site is largely underwater beneath Weiss Lake. Some accounts describe Turkeytown as a dispersed zone along the Coosa River, and a minority of sources place elements closer to Gadsden, but "near Gadsden" is misleading.

Sources:
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkeytown_(Cherokee_town)
- https://gadsdenmessenger.com/fort-turkeytown-location-remains-an-etowah-mystery/
- https://encyclopediaofalabama.org/article/centre/

## Suzanne Silvercruys nationality
**Claim in article:** "a Belgian sculptor named Suzanne Silvercruys"
**Verdict:** Correct. Born in Maaseik, Belgium in 1898. Represented Belgium in the 1932 LA Olympics. Immigrated to the U.S. and became a citizen in 1922 — "Belgian-American" is more precise, but "Belgian" is defensible.

Note: The official Noccalula Falls Park website calls her "originally a Norwegian heiress" — this is flatly wrong. She had no Norwegian connection. "Belgian" vs. "Belgian-born" considered and resolved — "Belgian" stays.

Sources:
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suzanne_Silvercruys
- https://olympics.com/en/athletes/suzanne-silvercruys-farnam-stevenson

## Cherokee etymology "ama uqwalelvyi"
**Claim in article:** "is sometimes traced to the Cherokee term *ama uqwalelvyi*, roughly 'place where water thunders'"
**Verdict:** Uncertain. Changed from "likely comes from" to "is sometimes traced to."

The phrase *ama uqwalelvyi* is real and means roughly "tumbling waters" (*ama* = water in Cherokee). However, it is primarily documented as the etymology of Amicalola Falls in Dawson County, Georgia — not Noccalula. The Wikipedia article on Noccalula Falls Park uses this phrase, but it's a hypothesis, not confirmed by primary sources. May be a borrowed etymology that migrated from Amicalola into Noccalula lore.

Sources:
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noccalula_Falls_Park
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amicalola_Falls
- https://www.bhamwiki.com/w/Noccalula

## Thomas McClung (1845) and Faxon carving (1859)
**Claim in article:** "Thomas McClung claimed the falls and 40 acres around them in 1845. A man named Faxon carved his name into the rock wall behind the waterfall in 1859, and it's still there."
**Verdict:** Both confirmed.

McClung verified as first European-descent claimant by Encyclopedia of Alabama and the Gadsden Messenger. Faxon identified as a surveyor who drew a map for the C.R. Smith Company and carved his name and date on the rock wall — confirmed by the Gadsden Messenger and Wikipedia independently.

Sources:
