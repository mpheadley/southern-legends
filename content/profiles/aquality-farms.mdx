## The Warehouse

The building at 1414 Wilmer Avenue has been in the Sawyer family for two decades. It used to be The Produce Company, a wholesale operation that supplied local schools, restaurants, and hospitals. When Sam and John Mark decided to bring the space back to life, they kept the mission and changed the method. At the start of 2024, they began renovating the old warehouse into a soil-free urban farm using hydroponic vertical tower systems and controlled-environment mushroom cultivation.

The front of the farm is devoted to lettuce and microgreens. Vertical towers grow heads of lettuce in six-week cycles. No soil, no pesticides. Just water, nutrients, and light. 24,000 heads per cycle in a space most people would call a garage.

The back is where they grow gourmet and functional mushrooms: Lion's Mane, Oyster, Chestnut, and Reishi. Lion's Mane has research behind it for cognitive function. Turkey Tail and Reishi are used in immune support. Sam knows the science and he'll tell you about it, because what they're growing has real wellness applications that are only getting more attention.

## The Mission

What makes Aquality Farms more than a farming experiment is what they do with what they grow. They're partnered with the Alabama Farm to School Program, supplying pesticide-free produce to over 20,000 children every week. That's a significant portion of the school-age kids in the region eating food that was grown downtown, without chemicals, by two brothers who decided their family's old warehouse should mean something.

Their storefront sells direct to the public. Fresh produce, herbs, mushroom tinctures, ultrasonic mushroom extracts, and microgreen powders. They also put together a "Harvest Box" in collaboration with other local farms and businesses. That kind of cooperative move is what makes a local food economy actually work.

Aquality Farms is Sweet Grown Alabama certified. For a farm that operates without a single acre of land, that says something about where agriculture is heading.
