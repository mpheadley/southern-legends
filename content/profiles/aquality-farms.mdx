Sam Sawyer is my friend, so let me be upfront about that. But the reason I'm writing about Aquality Farms isn't friendship — it's that what he and his brother John Mark are doing in a warehouse on Wilmer Avenue is one of the most interesting things happening in downtown Anniston right now, and most people in town have no idea it's there.

The Sawyer brothers are growing 24,000 heads of lettuce and 300 pounds of gourmet mushrooms a week. In downtown Anniston. In 2,000 square feet. If that sounds impossible, you haven't seen what hydroponic farming looks like when someone actually commits to it.

## The Warehouse

The building at 1414 Wilmer Avenue has been in the Sawyer family for two decades. It used to be The Produce Company — a wholesale operation that serviced local schools, restaurants, and hospitals. When Sam and John Mark decided to resurrect the space, they kept the mission and changed the method. At the start of 2024, they began renovating the old warehouse into something entirely new: a soil-free urban farm using hydroponic vertical tower systems and controlled-environment mushroom cultivation.

The front of the farm is devoted to lettuce and microgreens. Vertical towers grow heads of lettuce in six-week cycles — no soil, no pesticides, just water, nutrients, and light. The math is staggering: 24,000 heads per cycle in a space most people would call a garage.

The back of the farm is where it gets interesting. That's where they grow gourmet and functional mushrooms — Lion's Mane, Oyster, Chestnut, and Reishi. If you don't know what functional mushrooms are, you will soon. Lion's Mane has research behind it for cognitive function. Turkey Tail and Reishi are used in immune support. Sam will talk your ear off about the science, and he should — the wellness applications of what they're growing are real and growing faster than the mushrooms themselves.

## The Mission

What makes Aquality Farms more than a cool farming experiment is what they do with what they grow. They're partnered with the Alabama Farm to School Program, supplying pesticide-free produce to over 20,000 children every week. That's not a rounding error. That's a significant portion of the school-age kids in the region eating food that was grown downtown, without chemicals, by two brothers who decided their family's old warehouse should mean something.

Their storefront — opened with a ribbon cutting attended by community members and local officials — sells direct to the public. Fresh produce, herbs, mushroom tinctures, ultrasonic mushroom extracts, and microgreen powders. They also put together a "Harvest Box" in collaboration with other local farms and businesses, which is the kind of cooperative move that makes a local food economy actually work.

Aquality Farms is Sweet Grown Alabama certified, which means they've met the state's standards for locally grown agricultural products. For a farm that operates without a single acre of land, that certification says something about where agriculture is heading.

## Why This Matters for Anniston

I've written about downtown Anniston in several profiles on this site — the Peerless Saloon, the Freedom Riders monument, the Kress building. Most of those stories are about preserving what was here. Aquality Farms is about building something new.

Downtown Anniston has been through cycles of decline and revival for decades. Every new business that opens on Noble Street or Gurnee Avenue or Wilmer Avenue is a bet that the revitalization is real. Sam and John Mark made that bet with a warehouse, some vertical towers, and a mushroom lab. And they're winning — not in the flashy, ribbon-cutting sense (though they did that too), but in the pounds-of-produce-per-week sense. The kind of winning that feeds kids.

<PullQuote>They made that bet with a warehouse, some vertical towers, and a mushroom lab. And they're winning.</PullQuote>

I've known Sam long enough to know he's not doing this for press coverage. He's doing it because he believes Anniston deserves access to clean, locally grown food, and because he saw an opportunity in a building his family already owned to make that happen. That combination — conviction plus pragmatism — is what every small business needs and most don't have.

The storefront is open Monday through Friday, 9 to 5:30, and Saturdays 10 to 4. Go see it. Buy some mushroom extract. Ask Sam about Lion's Mane. You'll leave knowing more than you did, and you'll have supported a farm that's feeding 20,000 kids a week from the middle of downtown.
