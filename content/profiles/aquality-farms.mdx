I met Sam Sawyer at the downtown Anniston farmers market. We were both vendors — Heather and I selling flowers, Sam selling produce. He became a business partner when we started providing flowers for his Harvest Box program. He'd come by the farm weekly to pick up flowers for the box, and he shared mushroom compost with us. We both partnered with Called Coffee to sell our stuff in Jerod's coffee shop. The flower farm closed, but we keep up with Sam's business posts and still bump into him around town.

But the reason I'm writing about Aquality Farms isn't that connection. It's that what he and his brother John Mark are doing in a warehouse on Wilmer Avenue is one of the most interesting things happening in downtown Anniston right now, and most people in town have no idea it's there.

The Sawyer brothers are growing 24,000 heads of lettuce and 300 pounds of gourmet mushrooms a week. In downtown Anniston. In a 6,000-square-foot converted warehouse. Sam studied horticulture at Auburn. His brother John Mark grew up in the same family business. Between the two of them, they turned a building that used to move other people's produce into a farm that grows its own.

## The Warehouse

The building at 1414 Wilmer Avenue has been in the Sawyer family for decades. Three generations in Anniston. It used to be The Produce Company, a wholesale operation that supplied local schools, restaurants, and hospitals. When Sam and John Mark decided to bring the space back to life, they kept the mission and changed the method. At the start of 2024, they began renovating. By April 2025, they cut the ribbon on the storefront.

The front 2,000 square feet is devoted to lettuce and microgreens. Vertical towers grow heads of lettuce in six-week cycles. No soil, no pesticides. Just water, nutrients, and light. 24,000 heads per cycle.

The back 1,000 square feet is where they grow gourmet and functional mushrooms: Lion's Mane, Oyster, Chestnut, and Reishi. Lion's Mane has research behind it for cognitive function. Turkey Tail and Reishi are used in immune support. Sam knows the science and he'll tell you about it, because what they're growing has real wellness applications that are only getting more attention.

## The Mission

What makes Aquality Farms more than a farming experiment is what they do with what they grow. They're partnered with the Alabama Farm to School Program, supplying pesticide-free produce to over 35,000 students. {/* CONFIRM WITH SAM: their website says 35,000, earlier press said 20,000. Which number is current? */} Kids in the region eating food that was grown downtown, without chemicals, by two brothers who decided their family's old warehouse should mean something.

Their storefront sells direct to the public. Fresh produce, herbs, mushroom tinctures, ultrasonic mushroom extracts, and microgreen powders. They also put together a "Harvest Box" in collaboration with other local farms and businesses. That kind of cooperative move is what makes a local food economy actually work.

Aquality Farms is a Sweet Grown Alabama member. Sam is a charter member of the Northeast Alabama Wellness Alliance. For a farm that operates without a single acre of land, that says something about where agriculture is heading.

## Why This Matters for Anniston

Downtown Anniston has been through cycles of decline and revival for decades. Every new business that opens on Wilmer Avenue is a bet that the revitalization is real.
