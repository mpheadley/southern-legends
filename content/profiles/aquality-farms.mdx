<ArticleImage src="/images/profiles/aquality-farms/sam-farmers-market.webp" alt="Samuel Sawyer at the farmers market with mushrooms and tinctures at the Aquality Farms booth" caption="Samuel at the farmers market. This is where we met." layout="right" width={1152} height={2048} />

Samuel Sawyer showed up at the downtown Anniston farmers market one Saturday morning with a pair of big white coolers and no booth reputation. I'd been shopping the market for years and helping Heather sell flowers there for a couple by then. He hauled everything in by hand. Mushrooms, lettuce, tinctures. Had a juice he'd offer samples of. My kids begged for it every week after that.

People started lining up at the booth. Not just to buy, but also to ask questions. Samuel would pull a mushroom out of the cooler and start explaining how it grew. John Mark would already be talking to the next person in line. They never rushed anybody.

We became business partners. Heather and I grew flowers for his Harvest Box, he shared mushroom compost with us. I miss those Saturday mornings.

But I didn't know what I was watching start. Samuel told me once the whole thing began with notes on a napkin. What I didn't realize was that napkin had five years of research behind it before he ever set up those coolers.

What he and his brother built from that plan is one of the most interesting things happening in downtown Anniston right now. They're growing 24,000 heads of lettuce and 300 pounds of gourmet mushrooms a week. In a 6,000-square-foot converted warehouse on Wilmer Avenue.

## The Warehouse

The building at 1414 Wilmer Avenue has been in the Sawyer family for thirty years. Three generations in Anniston. Their grandparents ran Sawyer Anniston Pharmacy from the late sixties until 1999. Their father Mark ran a produce business out of the same building. When Samuel and John Mark decided to bring the space back to life, they kept the mission and changed the method.

<ArticleImage src="/images/profiles/aquality-farms/sawyer-brothers-grow-room.webp" alt="Samuel and John Mark Sawyer standing in the Aquality Farms grow room with vertical lettuce towers" caption="Samuel and John Mark in the grow room. Both brothers run Aquality Farms together." layout="center" width={2048} height={1536} />

They spent six years studying hydroponics before they touched a wall. At the start of 2024, they began renovating. By April 2025, they cut the ribbon on the storefront.

The front 2,000 square feet is devoted to lettuce and microgreens. Vertical towers grow heads of lettuce in six-week cycles. No soil, no pesticides. Just water, nutrients, and light. 24,000 heads per cycle.

<ArticleImage src="/images/profiles/aquality-farms/lettuce-under-lights.webp" alt="Lettuce growing under colored LED grow lights at Aquality Farms" caption="Lettuce under the grow lights. No soil, no sunlight, no pesticides." layout="full" width={2560} height={1920} />

The back 1,000 square feet is the mushroom lab. Lion's Mane, Oyster, Chestnut, Reishi. They started 2026 by building a new fruiting chamber: coated floors, FRP walls, blue-spectrum lights that trigger pinning earlier. A new incubation room is going in now. Samuel knows the science behind every species in there, and he'll talk about it if you let him.

<ArticleImage src="/images/profiles/aquality-farms/mushroom-grow-kits.webp" alt="Aquality Farms mushroom grow kits: Blue Oyster, Italian Oyster, and Black Pearl" caption="Blue Oyster, Italian Oyster, Black Pearl. Boxed and ready to grow at home." layout="left" width={1200} height={900} />

They're also expanding outside. A fence is going up on the south side of the property for outdoor growing systems. More lettuce, more capacity, less warehouse ceiling.

## The Mission

At the center of everything they do is a single word: health. Health for the individual. Health for the community. Health for the environment. Aquality Farms and Chaha Outdoors are much more than businesses — they're access points. To natural wellness products. To a different perspective on what's possible. Most importantly, to a community that actually listens and supports.

They're partnered with the Alabama Farm to School Program, supplying pesticide-free produce to over 35,000 students across the region, with expansion plans underway.

<ArticleImage src="/images/profiles/aquality-farms/products-spread.webp" alt="Aquality Farms products: butter lettuce, microgreens, oyster mushrooms, and mushroom tincture bottles" caption="What comes out of the warehouse: lettuce, microgreens, oyster mushrooms, and their own line of tinctures." layout="right" width={2048} height={1536} />

The storefront sells direct to the public. Fresh produce, herbs, mushroom tinctures, ultrasonic mushroom extracts, microgreen powders. They also put together a "Harvest Box" with other local farms and businesses.

<ArticleImage src="/images/profiles/aquality-farms/harvest-box.webp" alt="Aquality Farms Harvest Box with Golden Oyster mushrooms, butter lettuce, microgreens, sweet potatoes, and other local farm products" caption="The Harvest Box: Golden Oyster mushrooms, lettuce, microgreens, and produce from other local farms. One box, multiple growers." layout="left" width={1536} height={2048} />

<ArticleImage src="/images/profiles/aquality-farms/samuel-john-mark.webp" alt="Samuel and John Mark Sawyer at the Aquality Farms booth with their mushroom grow kits and products" caption="Samuel and John Mark at a market booth with their products. Two brothers, one mission." layout="right" width={5712} height={4284} />

This year they launched a School Sponsorship Program, letting local businesses and organizations fund pesticide-free produce for students directly. Growing the food was the first problem. Paying for it to reach kids was the second. The sponsorship program solves the second one.

Aquality Farms is a Sweet Grown Alabama member. No acreage. No land at all. Just a warehouse and a method.

When I asked Samuel if there was ever a moment where it all felt too risky or too big, his answer was simple: "The hardest part was getting over the fear of failing, the perception of failure by others." He paused. "But every time I have failed in my life it has made me stronger and the man I am today. That prevents a large majority of us from taking risk and it doesn't matter. What matters is doing what we know is right, what we are called to do and the rest will take care of itself."

He said it like he'd learned it the hard way. Like failure had taught him something most people never get to know.

## Chaha Outdoors

Samuel also runs Chaha Outdoors. The name comes from the Creek word for "high place," the same root as Cheaha Mountain, thirty minutes from Anniston.

<ArticleImage src="/images/profiles/aquality-farms/sam-chaha-trail.webp" alt="Samuel Sawyer on a trail run in Calhoun County" caption="Same guy, different setting. Samuel on the trails through Chaha Outdoors." layout="right" width={960} height={720} />

Samuel leads wellness walks, shows up at races with the Anniston Runners Club, and talks up every trail in Calhoun County to anyone who'll listen. Chief Ladiga, Coldwater Mountain, Pinhoti.

The farm grows food that's good for you. Chaha gets you outside. One operates from a warehouse. The other from a trailhead. Both are part of the same mission: health for the individual, for the community, for the environment.

Downtown Anniston has been through cycles of decline and revival for decades. Every new business that opens downtown is a bet that the revitalization is real. Samuel and John Mark made that bet with a warehouse, some vertical towers, and a mushroom lab. And they're winning. Not in the flashy sense. In the pounds-of-produce-per-week sense. In the thousands of students eating food that will actually nourish them. In the trails getting walked and the community getting built.

For years I'd walk down Wilmer Avenue when I worked downtown. Walked right past that humble warehouse a hundred times. Had no idea what was growing in there.
