Every town has a bar with a story. Anniston has one with about six stories stacked on top of each other, and at least one ghost who refuses to let anyone forget.

The Peerless Saloon has been serving drinks at 15 West 10th Street since 1899. That makes it Alabama's oldest operating bar — a title it's earned not just by surviving, but by being the kind of place that accumulates history like dust on a whiskey bottle. A World's Fair bar, a brothel, a murder, Prohibition bootlegging, decades of abandonment, a restoration, a Netflix cameo, and a resident ghost. You can't make this stuff up. Anniston wouldn't let you.

## Daddy Garner's Saloon

Robert E. "Daddy" Garner came to Anniston from a farm in Georgia sometime in the late 1800s. When liquor became legal in Anniston in 1890, Garner saw his opportunity. He opened The Peerless in 1899 and built it into one of the fanciest watering holes in town — a Classic Revival Gothic building where his house blend, a whiskey called Old Wildcat, flowed for both working men and the city's business class.

Garner wasn't subtle about ambition. He bought old church pews, sawed them in half, and turned them into customer booths. And in 1904, he went to the St. Louis World's Fair and bought a massive mirror-backed mahogany bar — the kind of piece that turns a saloon into a destination. The bar was shipped to Anniston in 1905, reportedly on the same train that carried the Vulcan statue back to Birmingham. It's still there, in what's now called the Alabama Room, and it's still the centerpiece of the place.

## The Second Floor

A model city needs its vices, and Garner provided those too. The entire second floor of The Peerless operated as a brothel — four rooms, each with its own ornamented fireplace, plus a fifth bedroom in a loft accessed by a ladder. Presiding over it all was a woman named Lucinda Talley.

Lucinda ran the operation from a chair at the top of the stairs. She screened visitors, collected payment, and by most accounts ran a tight ship. She was known for carrying a gun and for gifting expensive ties to her best customers at Christmas. She sat at that post for more than twenty years — long enough to become as much a fixture of The Peerless as the mahogany bar downstairs.

## The Shooting

Around 1919 or 1920 — the sources disagree on the exact year — Anniston police chased a suspect into The Peerless. The man bolted up the stairs toward the brothel. An officer drew his weapon and fired. He missed the suspect. He hit Lucinda Talley instead, killing her at the top of the stairs — the same spot where she'd sat for two decades.

That's where the ghost story starts. Staff and owners over the years have reported seeing Lucinda at her post at the top of the stairs, hearing footsteps and disembodied voices on the second floor, watching glasses break behind the bar with nobody near them. Whether you believe in ghosts or not, the story has become inseparable from the bar itself. Lucinda Talley ran The Peerless in life, and by most accounts, she never stopped.

## Prohibition, Decline, and Resurrection

Anniston went dry well before the rest of the country — Alabama voted a statewide ban on alcohol in 1915. Garner apparently kept bootlegging through The Peerless until his death in 1919 at age 63. After that, the building went through a long, slow decline. Decades of abandonment left it gutted and decaying, the kind of building people walk past and wonder what it used to be.

In 1985, The Peerless was placed on the National Register of Historic Places. In 1992, it was restored and reopened. The mahogany bar survived. The bones of the building survived. And the stories — Daddy Garner, Lucinda Talley, Old Wildcat whiskey, the World's Fair — survived too.

More recently, scenes from the Netflix film *Devil All the Time* were filmed inside The Peerless, with Bill Skarsgard and Sebastian Stan on set. The building has a way of attracting stories.

## What It Is Now

I've been to The Peerless enough times to know it's more than a novelty. The food is solid, the atmosphere is genuinely unique, and the building itself is worth the visit even if you're not a history person. But it helps to know the history, because the place makes more sense when you do. The dark wood, the ornate bar, the staircase to the second floor — it all carries weight.

Downtown Anniston has been through cycles of decline and revival, and The Peerless has outlasted most of them. It's the kind of place that anchors a block, that gives a downtown district a story to tell beyond "we're working on it." When people visit Anniston and ask where to go first, this is the answer more often than not.

Alabama's oldest bar, a World's Fair relic, a ghost named Lucinda, and a pretty decent burger. You could do worse for a Tuesday night.
