Before my wife Heather and I ever put a flower in the ground at our farm in Anniston, we learned from Trevor and Joanna Mann. They run WaldenFARMacy — a small-scale permaculture herb farm out of Bessemer, Alabama — and they changed the way I think about land, growing, and what a farm is even supposed to be.

I came to them with a church background and a vague idea that farming meant planting things in rows and hoping for the best. They showed me something different. They showed me an ecosystem.

## Trevor and Joanna

Trevor Mann is the kind of person whose mind works in systems. When a friend introduced him to permaculture — the practice of designing farms and landscapes that mimic natural ecosystems — something clicked. He trained under Cliff Davis and Jennifer Albanese at Spiral Ridge Permaculture and graduated from the family herbalist program at the Appalachian School for Natural Health. He designs food forests, builds soil through cover cropping and rotational grazing, and thinks about a farm the way an ecologist thinks about a watershed.

Joanna Mann is a yogi, herbalist, energy healer, massage therapist, and mother — and yes, she's all of those things at once. She trained at Red Mountain Institute, studied yoga with Akasha Ellis at Birmingham Yoga, and earned herbal training from the Appalachian School for Natural Health alongside Trevor. She brings the healing side of what the farm produces — the knowledge of which plants do what, and how to turn a harvest into medicine.

Together, they grow herbs on their Bessemer property using permaculture methods and process them into small-batch herbal medicines: tinctures, syrups, salves, and teas. Their first tincture outsold all their vegetables and microgreens combined, and they had the sense to follow that signal. WaldenFARMacy was born from the intersection of what the land wanted to produce and what their community needed.

## What Permaculture Actually Means

I've heard permaculture described a hundred different ways, and most of them make it sound more complicated than it is. Here's how I understand it after learning from Trevor: instead of fighting the land to make it produce what you want, you design systems that work the way nature already works. Cover crops feed the soil. Animals rotate through pastures and fertilize as they go. Plants grow in guilds — combinations that support each other — rather than monoculture rows.

The result, at WaldenFARMacy, is herbs that are genuinely stronger. Trevor will tell you that plants grown in whole ecosystems — competing with other species, drawing nutrients from living soil — produce more potent medicine than anything grown in isolation. The growing practices that heal the land are the same ones that make the strongest product. That's not marketing. It's biology.

When Heather and I were building our flower farm, this was the lens Trevor and Joanna gave us. Don't fight the land. Work with it. Build the soil first. Let the ecosystem do what it knows how to do. We applied those principles to cut flowers instead of herbs, but the philosophy was the same — and it came from two people in Bessemer who had figured it out by paying attention.

## The Medicine

WaldenFARMacy's product line reads like a field guide to what grows in Alabama and what it can do. Elderberry syrups for immune support. Tinctures for everything from anxiety to inflammation. Salves made from plants harvested on the property or wildcrafted nearby. Everything small-batch, everything traceable to a specific piece of ground.

They sell at local markets, through their website, and through one-on-one herbal consultations. Joanna's background in bodywork and energy healing means the consultations go deeper than "take this tincture" — she's looking at the whole person, the whole system. Same philosophy as the farm: everything is connected.

They also donate a minimum of 1% of sales every quarter to a different nonprofit. It's not a huge number for a small farm, but it's built into the model from the beginning, not added as an afterthought.

## Why They Matter

There are a lot of people selling herbal products. There are a lot of people talking about permaculture. What makes Trevor and Joanna different — at least from where I sit — is that they live it. The farm isn't a brand exercise. It's their livelihood, their classroom, and the source of everything they sell. The medicine comes from the land. The land is managed by the principles. The principles were learned from mentors who learned them from the ecosystems themselves.

That chain — from soil to plant to medicine to community — is unbroken at WaldenFARMacy. It's the kind of integrity that's hard to fake and easy to recognize when you see it.

They taught me what they know because that's what they do. They share it. And somewhere in Anniston, on a farm that's since been sold, the soil Heather and I built using their methods is still doing its work. That's permaculture. The impact outlasts the farmer.
