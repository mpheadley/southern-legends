I showed up at Jacksonville State University as a kid who could sing and liked being on stage. I left with a B.A. in Music — voice concentration, drama minor — and a set of skills I didn't fully understand until years later, when I found myself standing in front of congregations and communities doing work that had nothing to do with opera and everything to do with what opera had taught me.

JSU's music and theater programs aren't the biggest in the state. They're not trying to be. What they are is close — close enough that your professors know your voice, your range, your tendencies, your potential. In Mason Hall, you're not a number in a lecture. You're a person in a practice room with a teacher who showed up because they care about the work.

## Mason Hall

The David L. Walters Department of Music lives in Mason Hall on JSU's campus. It's accredited by the National Association of Schools of Music, which means the training is rigorous — applied lessons, recitals every semester, theory, history, pedagogy, the full conservatory curriculum delivered in a small-university setting.

I studied voice. That meant semesters of individual lessons, jury performances, recitals, and the slow, humbling process of learning to use an instrument that lives inside your body. Voice pedagogy — understanding not just how to sing but how singing works, the mechanics and the science of it — turned out to be one of the most useful things I ever learned. Not because I became an opera singer, but because it taught me how to communicate. How to fill a room. How to hold attention with intention rather than volume.

The department also runs the Jax State Lyric Theatre and a community Music Academy that provides lessons in piano, voice, guitar, and strings to students across Calhoun County and beyond. The academy is one of those quiet programs that does enormous good — kids who might not otherwise have access to private music instruction getting trained by university faculty and advanced students.

## The Stone Center

The Department of Theatre and Film operates out of the Stone Center Theatre, and it's where I spent the other half of my undergraduate life. The drama minor meant acting classes, stage management, production work, and performances — Sweeney Todd, Pirates of Penzance, The Secret Garden, Jesus Christ Superstar. Each one a crash course in collaboration, timing, vulnerability, and the particular terror of walking onto a stage knowing that the only safety net is preparation.

The theater department offers B.A. degrees in both Theatre and Film, with concentrations in performance and production. Their production archive goes back to the 1940s, and the range of what they've staged — musicals, dramas, Shakespeare, contemporary work — reflects a department that takes its students seriously enough to challenge them.

They also partner with The Shakespeare Project, which introduces area high school students to Shakespeare's works for free. That kind of community outreach from a university theater department matters more than most people realize. For some of those kids, it's the first time anyone has told them that their voice belongs on a stage.

## What It Gave Me

Here's what I didn't understand when I was twenty: the performing arts aren't just about performing. They're about presence. About reading a room. About knowing when to be loud and when to be still. About showing up prepared and then letting go of the plan when the moment requires something else.

I used every one of those skills in nineteen years of ministry. Every sermon was a performance — not in the fake sense, but in the sense that I was standing in front of people trying to communicate something that mattered, using my voice and my body and my timing to make it land. Every funeral, every wedding, every Wednesday night Bible study. The tools I reached for were the ones Mason Hall and the Stone Center put in my hands.

Now I build websites and write stories about people and places in Northeast Alabama. Different medium, same impulse — take something true and make it reach people. JSU didn't just teach me to sing. It taught me to show up, to pay attention, and to believe that the work of communicating is worth doing well.

I'm still using what they gave me. Every day.

## The Program Now

JSU's performing arts programs continue to turn out musicians, educators, and theater artists from a campus in Jacksonville that most people associate with football before they think of Mason Hall. That's fine. The people who know, know. The department is small enough to see you and rigorous enough to push you, and that combination is rarer than it should be.

If you're a student in Northeast Alabama with talent and the willingness to work, JSU's music and theater programs are worth a serious look. And if you're a community member who hasn't attended a concert or production at the Stone Center, you're missing out. The tickets are cheap, the talent is real, and the building is fifteen minutes from wherever you are in Calhoun County.
