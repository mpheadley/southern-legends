In Calhoun County, there's no shortage of churches. Drive any stretch of highway between Anniston and Jacksonville and you'll pass Baptist, Methodist, Presbyterian, Catholic, AME, Church of Christ, nondenominational — the full spectrum of American faith, planted every quarter mile like fence posts. Most of the time, these congregations do their own thing. But Interfaith Ministries is what happens when they decide to do something together.

## The Model

The premise is simple enough that it almost doesn't need explaining: individual churches have limited budgets for helping people in crisis. But if dozens of congregations pool a portion of their benevolence funds into a single organization, that organization can serve far more people, more consistently, than any one church could on its own.

That's Interfaith Ministries in a sentence. The execution, of course, is more complicated. The organization provides emergency financial assistance — rent payments to prevent eviction, utility bills to keep the lights on, prescription costs for people choosing between medication and groceries. The needs are urgent and the math is unforgiving. A family that falls two months behind on rent doesn't need spiritual counsel. They need $800 before Friday.

## The People in the Middle

The staff and volunteers at Interfaith Ministries occupy an unusual position. They sit at the intersection of faith and bureaucracy, compassion and logistics. On any given day, they're reviewing applications, calling landlords, verifying utility accounts, and making decisions about how to allocate funds that are never quite enough to meet the demand.

It's social work dressed in church clothes — or maybe it's ministry dressed in spreadsheets. Either way, the people doing it will tell you the same thing: the paperwork exists to make sure the money goes where it's needed most, not to make people jump through hoops. There's a difference between accountability and gatekeeping, and Interfaith tries to stay on the right side of that line.

The member congregations themselves represent a quiet rebuke to the idea that churches can't work together. Baptist and Catholic, Black and white, downtown and rural — the coalition holds because the shared commitment to helping people outweighs the theological differences that might otherwise keep them in separate lanes.

## What Falls Through the Cracks

Every community has gaps in its safety net. Government programs have eligibility requirements and processing times. Nonprofit organizations have funding cycles and service areas. Churches have limited budgets and volunteer bandwidth. Interfaith Ministries exists in the space between all of these — the place where someone who doesn't quite qualify for one program and can't wait for another can still get help before the situation becomes a crisis.

This is the work that rarely makes the news. There's no ribbon-cutting when a family avoids eviction. No press release when someone's electricity stays on. The impact is measured in crises averted, in the things that didn't happen because someone intervened at the right moment with the right amount of money and the willingness to act fast.

## The Larger Point

Interfaith Ministries of Calhoun County isn't trying to solve poverty. The people who run it would be the first to tell you that. What they're trying to do is keep the floor from falling out from under people who are already standing on the edge. It's emergency work — triage, not treatment — and it depends entirely on the continued generosity of congregations that have chosen to see their neighbors' problems as their own.

In a county where the challenges are real and the resources are finite, that choice matters more than most people realize. The churches keep giving, the staff keeps showing up, and the doors stay open. It's not a solution. It's a commitment. And in Calhoun County, that's often the difference between falling and not.
