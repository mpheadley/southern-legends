There's a moment on the Chief Ladiga Trail, somewhere between Jacksonville and Piedmont, where the farmland opens up and the foothills rise in front of you and you forget you're on a former railroad bed in Calhoun County, Alabama. You're just moving. The pavement is smooth, the grade is gentle, and the next ten miles look exactly like the kind of ride you'd plan a weekend around.

That's the Chief Ladiga. Nearly 40 miles of paved trail from Anniston to the Georgia state line, winding through Weaver, Jacksonville, and Piedmont. Connect to the Silver Comet Trail on the other side and you've got 105 miles of unbroken pavement stretching to the suburbs of Atlanta. It's the longest paved pedestrian pathway in the United States, and it started with one small town and a conservation grant.

## How Piedmont Started Something

The trail began in 1990, when the City of Piedmont received a Land and Water Conservation Grant to purchase an unused rail corridor through Piedmont and Cleburne County. That's it — one grant, one small city, one vision for what an abandoned railroad could become.

It took years of planning, construction, and incremental expansion to push the trail from Piedmont west through Jacksonville and into Anniston. In 2008, the Chief Ladiga was connected to Georgia's Silver Comet Trail at the state line, and in 2009 the combined route was inducted into the Rails to Trails Conservancy's Hall of Fame.

In April 2025, Anniston celebrated the opening of a 6.5-mile extension that brings the trail to the city's Amtrak depot — turning Anniston into a true endpoint and potential starting hub for people tackling both trails.

## The Ride

The surface is asphalt, the grade is mostly gentle, and the trail gains about 700 feet of elevation over its full length as you head east toward Georgia. The toughest climbing is between Piedmont and the state line, where you'll rise from about 680 feet to 1,000 feet over nine miles — but spread out over that distance, even that is manageable for most riders.

The trail passes through farms, forests, wetlands, and the foothills of the southern Appalachians. It crosses through the downtowns of four communities, each with its own character. Anniston is the urban bookend. Jacksonville — where I went to college at JSU and where I'm based now — is the college town. Weaver, where I pastored a church for seven years, connects the two. And Piedmont — Piedmont is the trail town.

## Piedmont: Where the Trails Meet

Downtown Piedmont sits at a convergence that most cities would kill for: the Chief Ladiga Trail, the Pinhoti Trail, and Terrapin Creek all come together here. Bike, hike, or paddle — pick your adventure and park once. The businesses along Main Street know exactly who their customers are, and they've built around it.

**Pinhoti Pizza Company** sits at the intersection of the trail and Main Street — literally where the trail crosses the downtown grid. Brick oven pizza, sandwiches, craft beer, and a location that couldn't be better if you designed the town around the restaurant. Which, in a sense, they did.

**Elevated Grounds** is a coffee shop steps from the trail — espresso, smoothies, breakfast, and the kind of atmosphere that makes cyclists feel welcome rather than tolerated. Trail-user support isn't an afterthought here. It's the business model.

**Ladiga Trail Trikes** is Neal Gladden's second location — he runs Rocket City Trikes in Huntsville and opened the Piedmont shop because the trail traffic justified it. Recumbent trikes, cycling accessories, and the kind of specialized retail that only shows up where demand is real.

**Main Street South** is a 10,000-square-foot vendor mall and mercantile. The Eubanks Welcome Center greets visitors near a mural featuring a map of the Chief Ladiga Trail. It's the kind of cluster of trail-friendly businesses that turns a rest stop into a destination.

## The Economic Argument

This isn't just a nice place to ride a bike. Jacksonville State University's Center for Economic Development and Business Research studied four major trails in Calhoun County and found $9.7 million in total economic output in 2023. The trails bring visitors, visitors spend money, and that money flows into restaurants, shops, gas stations, and hotels across the county.

Audrey Maxwell, tourism director for the Calhoun County Area Chamber and Visitors Center, has called the impact "huge in terms of recreation and visitation." And the numbers are growing — the 2025 trail extension, the Coldwater Mountain development, and Alabama's designation of 2025 as the Year of Alabama Trails are all pushing the region's outdoor recreation profile higher.

I build websites for local businesses, and I can tell you that "outdoor recreation destination" is a search category that Calhoun County is starting to own. Five years ago, that wasn't true. The trail is a big part of why it's true now.

## The Practical Stuff

Most cyclists ride the Chief Ladiga as an out-and-back. There's good support — access points, parking, nearby businesses — from Anniston through Piedmont. Past Piedmont toward the state line, services thin out, so plan accordingly. The trail accommodates walking, running, cycling, and inline skating. In Cleburne County, equestrians are welcome too.

If you're ambitious, the full Chief Ladiga plus Silver Comet ride is about 105 miles one way. Reviewers consistently say the same thing: mind the weather, load up in Piedmont, and enjoy one of the flattest century-plus rides you'll ever find. It's not the mountains. It's the foothills. And the foothills are just right.
