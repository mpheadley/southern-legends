There's a 50-foot stone tower standing in a park outside Ohatchee that most people in Calhoun County have never visited. It's not a chimney, not a silo, not a monument in the traditional sense. It's what's left of a Confederate iron furnace that was built to help win a war and destroyed before it ever produced a single pound of iron.

The story of Janney Furnace is one of those Civil War episodes that's stranger and sadder than anything you'd put in a novel.

## The Builder

In early 1863, a Montgomery businessman named Alfred A. Janney came to Calhoun County — then still called Benton County — to buy pig iron and ore for his foundry. Janney's Montgomery operation specialized in ordnance for the Confederacy. Cannons, shot, the tools of war. He needed raw material, and he'd been buying from the Cane Creek Furnace, an ironworks that had operated in the area since 1840.

While scouting north of Ohatchee, Janney noticed iron ore scattered across the ground. The location was ideal: abundant ore, nearby limestone for flux, dense woods for charcoal, and the Coosa River close enough to ship product at high water. When the river ran low, iron could go by wagon to a railroad terminus 16 miles away in Anniston.

Janney bought the land from a local farmer, bought out his initial partner, and began construction.

## The Construction

What Janney built was ambitious. Workers excavated into the south side of a ridge, built a massive retaining wall, and leveled the hilltop for a stocking yard. Enslaved stonecutters shaped sandstone blocks — some weighing over a ton — by hand and stacked them 50 feet high. The furnace was designed to produce 15 tons of cast iron per day.

That detail is worth sitting with. The furnace that stands in Janney Park today was built by enslaved people, cutting and shaping stone by hand, to produce iron for weapons that would be used to fight a war to keep them enslaved. The structure itself is a monument to both extraordinary craftsmanship and extraordinary cruelty — and you can't honestly look at it without holding both of those things at once.

## The Destruction

The furnace was completed and ready to fire when history arrived ahead of schedule.

On July 14, 1864, a Union cavalry force of 2,300 men under Major General Lovell H. Rousseau crossed the Coosa River at Ten Islands Ford. Rousseau sent a detail under Engineer Officer Ed Ruger to the area's ironworks. They hit the Cane Creek Furnace first and obliterated it. Then they reached Janney Furnace.

Ruger's men demolished the superstructure and burned everything that would light. The furnace never produced a single pound of pig iron. Months of construction, tons of hand-cut stone, the labor of enslaved workers — all of it rendered pointless in an afternoon by a cavalry raid.

Janney later tried to salvage equipment — pulleys, wheels, boilers — but never attempted to rebuild. The war ended less than a year later.

## What Remains

The stone stack is still there. That's the remarkable thing. The Union cavalry destroyed the superstructure, but they couldn't destroy 50 feet of hand-cut sandstone. It stands in Janney Furnace Park, maintained by the Calhoun County Parks and Recreation Office, looking exactly like what it is: a ruin with a story bigger than itself.

The park includes a Confederate Museum and a Native American Museum with over 500 artifacts — a reminder that this land has layers of history that go back far beyond the Civil War. The furnace was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1976.

## Why I Write About It

I write about this place because it's complicated, and complicated places are the ones worth writing about. Janney Furnace isn't a celebration of anything. It's not a feel-good story about community or resilience. It's a stone tower in a park that forces you to think about who built it, why they built it, and what it cost — in every sense of that word.

The Appalachian foothills are full of places like this. Beautiful, quiet, and carrying a weight that you only feel if you know the history. Janney Furnace is one of them. It's worth the drive to Ohatchee, and it's worth standing in front of that stack for a few minutes and letting the story settle.
