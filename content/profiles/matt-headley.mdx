For nineteen years, my life made sense. I ran Headley Flower Farm in Anniston, Alabama — growing cut flowers, working the soil, selling at markets and to local florists. I served as a pastor and church music director, building worship services and leading congregations through the kinds of moments that shape a community: weddings, funerals, Wednesday nights, Sunday mornings. From 2006 to 2025 — nineteen years of showing up, planting things, and watching them grow.

I loved the work. Not in a résumé way — in the way that you love something that becomes the architecture of your days. The creativity of arranging a service or a flower bed. The slow trust that builds between a pastor and a congregation. The satisfaction of harvesting something you put in the ground months ago and handing it to somebody who lights up when they see it.

Then it all came apart.

## The Middle of the Story

I live with bipolar disorder. For years, I managed it — the way a lot of people manage things they don't talk about, with effort and routine and the quiet hope that the ground will stay solid. Then came a manic episode that wasn't quiet. It was public, and it was devastating.

<PullQuote>You don't lose one thing. You lose the version of yourself that held everything together.</PullQuote>

I lost the ministry. I lost the farm. I lost relationships, credibility, and the daily rhythm that had defined my adult life. Not gradually, the way careers sometimes wind down — all at once, in the kind of collapse that makes people in a small town look at you differently when you walk into the grocery store.

There's no softening that. It happened. And for a while, the only honest answer to "what do you do?" was "I'm figuring that out."

## Building in Public

What I had, underneath the rubble, was a set of skills I'd been using for years without thinking of them as a career. I'd built websites for my church and my flower farm business. I understood how to make something clear, functional, and honest — how to take what a person or organization actually does and put it in front of the people who need to find it. I just hadn't called it a profession yet.

So I started [Headley Web & SEO](https://headleyweb.com). Not with a grand launch or a marketing budget — with the kind of stubborn, one-foot-in-front-of-the-other energy that comes from having no other option. I build websites for local service businesses: plumbers, contractors, HVAC companies, the people whose work keeps Northeast Alabama running but whose online presence usually doesn't do them justice.

<PullQuote>Starting over doesn't mean starting from nothing. It means finding out what you had all along that you never thought to count.</PullQuote>

I'm good at it. Not because I went to school for it — because nineteen years of ministry taught me how to listen to what people actually need, and years of farming taught me that nothing grows without patience and consistent work. The technical skills are real, but the instinct for people is what makes the sites work.

## Why Local Legends Exists

Then I built this site.

Local Legends started as a portfolio project — a way to show potential clients what I could do with editorial design and storytelling. But it became something else. It became the thing that made the rebuilding feel like more than just survival.

The idea is simple: Northeast Alabama is full of people and places whose stories don't get told. Not because they aren't worth telling, but because nobody's writing them down. The outreach center that feeds hundreds of families. The museum that rivals institutions in cities ten times Anniston's size. The nonprofits running on volunteer hours and stubbornness. I know what it feels like to be invisible — to have your story reduced to the worst chapter. So I write the stories of places and people who deserve to be seen for what they actually are.

<PullQuote>I know what it feels like to be invisible — to have your story reduced to the worst chapter.</PullQuote>

It's not charity. It's not therapy, though it probably helps with that too. It's community building — the same work I did as a pastor, just in a different form. Instead of a pulpit, it's a website. Instead of a congregation, it's a town. The impulse is the same: find the people doing good work, and make sure somebody notices.

## What Comes Next

I'm not going to pretend the hard part is over. Bipolar disorder doesn't go away because you found a new career. Rebuilding a reputation in a small town takes longer than building one in the first place. There are still days when the grocery store feels like a gauntlet.

But I'm building. The web business is growing. Local Legends is publishing real stories about real places. And every profile I write is a small act of defiance against the idea that your worst moment gets to define you — mine included.

If you know me, you probably know some version of this story already. This is the version I'd like you to hear: not the fall, but what I built after it. And if you know someone in Northeast Alabama whose story deserves to be told, I'd love to hear about them.
