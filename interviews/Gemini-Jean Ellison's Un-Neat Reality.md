**The View from the Boat: Jean Ellison and the Music Box**

"Am I late for practice? What is it? No. No, no, no. We’d be calling you Matt," Jean says as soon as she picks up the phone.

Even through the speaker, her voice carries that sharp, rhythmic warmth of a choir director who has seen you at your most disorganized. She’s referencing a version of me from nearly twenty years ago at Jacksonville State University—a version that was, in my own words, a "sketchball". It’s a comfortable, old-friend kind of opening, the kind that makes you think you know exactly where the story is going.

But you don’t know what happened between then and now.

Jean had the plan. It was a perfect, Type-A, Southeast-Alabama-success-story plan: finish the degrees, marry the pastor, have the baby, and land the dream job. In August, right as she was starting at Weaver High School, she had her son, Kingston.

"When you plan, God laughs," she tells me.

For Jean, that laughter sounded like a silence so heavy she couldn’t leave her house. It wasn’t the postpartum depression people usually talk about; it was a hyper-vigilant, razor-edged anxiety. She was the only one who could care for the baby. If someone else touched him, they were bringing germs. They were going to give him something "brain-eating". She nursed him all day and all night until her husband, Steve, finally told her, "Ain't no baby in this world that hungry, Jean".

She tried to white-knuckle it. She’d wake up at 5:00 AM to nurse, then leave at 6:00 AM to teach choir to seventy-five teenagers.

Then came the dissociation.

"The walls started closing in," she says. "Literally, I was in the classroom, and I thought I was on a boat".

The hospital called it sleep exhaustion. Jean’s response was pure, stubborn denial: "I’m Type A. I don’t get exhausted". But the exhaustion didn’t care about her personality type. Soon, she couldn't work. She couldn't walk. The income stopped, and the "perfect plan" began to peel away in the form of a car repossession—a brand-new Altima with a push-start, the kind of car you buy when you’ve both earned your Master's and think you’ve finally made it.

She spent her days in the nursery, refusing to let anyone see her. When her best friend would come over, Jean would spiral into a panic. The friend—a newly minted therapist—would just tell her to lay on her back on the floor and talk her through the air.

It is from that floor, and from the loss of that dream job at Weaver, that The Music Box was born.

People look at Jean now and see the "bubbly and social" version she’s always been. They see a successful business owner and a parent "crushing it". But Jean doesn't want the sanitized version of her resilience. She doesn't want to be a symbol. She wants to be a person who takes her medicine and remembers what it felt like when her legs wouldn't work.

She’s still building, but she’s building with the knowledge that the ground can move. When her second son, Maverick, was born, she didn't wait for the boat to start rocking. Six hours after delivery, she looked at the doctors and told them, "I need to start my regimen".

"Welcome to the club," she told me when I shared my own rough year. "We are all okay".

It’s not a brochure-ready ending. There’s no "and then she was never sad again." There is just Jean, taking her medicine, running her business, and making sure that if life decides to laugh at her plans again, she’s already standing on her own two feet.
