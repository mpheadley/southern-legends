## Jean Ellison Is Still Building

“Am I late for practice?”

She says it before anything else—already laughing, already calling me out.

Not a real question. A callback.

We’re back at Jacksonville State for a second, standing in a hallway, me walking in late like I always did, her clocking it before I can say anything.

“No,” I tell her. “We’d be calling you Matt.”

She remembers. That’s the first thing that lands. Not the years, not the distance, not everything that’s happened since. Just the rhythm of how we used to talk.

It would be easy to stay there. To let the conversation sit in that shared past where everything felt like it was still in motion, still becoming something. But it doesn’t stay there long.

Because what you don’t see, if you only know Jean now, is what happened between then and this.

---

She did everything right.

College. Degree. The next degree. A job lined up before the baby even arrived. Kingston in July. Classroom in August. The plan was clean. Linear. The kind of plan that feels like proof you’ve done what you were supposed to do.

Then it didn’t hold.

“I had never heard of postpartum anxiety,” she says. “I had heard of postpartum depression. I didn’t know this existed.”

It wasn’t sadness. It was vigilance. The kind that doesn’t turn off.

No one else could hold the baby. No one else could be trusted to care for him. Hands weren’t clean enough. Air wasn’t safe enough. The world narrowed down to a single task: keep him alive.

She didn’t sleep. Not really. Up all night, then up again before dawn to teach a full day.

At some point the body stops negotiating.

“I thought I was on a boat,” she says. Standing in her classroom, the walls shifting, the floor unsteady. Not metaphor. Not exaggeration. That’s what it felt like.

A doctor finally names it. Exhaustion. Anxiety. Something with edges and a shape to it, even if it still doesn’t feel manageable.

By then, things are already slipping.

She can’t work. No income. The car goes. Then more. The kind of losses that stack quietly until you look up and realize how much is gone.

“I never, ever, ever judge,” she says. “People have no idea.”

---

The help that mattered didn’t look like a system. It looked like one person showing up.

Her friend Lisa comes over. Jean doesn’t want her there. Doesn’t want anyone there.

“Don’t get near me.”

Lisa gets closer anyway. Lies down on the floor.

“Lay on your back,” she tells her.

And then again.

“Lay on your back.”

She talks her through it. One panic attack at a time.

It’s not a turning point. Not clean like that. But it’s a place where something starts to shift.

“Gene, your legs do work,” Lisa tells her later.

At first, she doesn’t believe her.

---

There’s a version of this story where the business comes later, once everything is stable again.

That’s not what happened.

There was no stable.

There was just a gap where income used to be, and a sense that something had to be done.

“I could just teach music,” she says. “I don’t have to be in a classroom.”

The idea is simple enough to miss. Not a grand pivot. Not a reinvention. Just a small adjustment to what she already knew how to do.

She writes a business plan. Ten pages. Hands it to Steve.

He looks at her. Takes it in. Doesn’t dismiss it.

The Music Box starts there. Not as a brand, not as a vision statement. As a way to keep going.

“That’s how it started,” she says. “Through my postpartum anxiety.”

---

It grows. Slowly at first, then faster.

A space on Noble Street. Students. Classes. A rhythm.

Then December comes and the building is sold out from under them.

“They sold it right from under us,” she says.

There’s a moment where it feels like that might be it. Not dramatically. Just practically. Lease gone. Space gone. No clear next step.

“Is this how it ends?” she remembers thinking.

It doesn’t.

They find another spot. Also on Noble. Better, she says. Bigger. The kind of move that only makes sense after the fact.

“We’ve grown exponentially.”

She says it plainly. Not as a claim. Just a statement of what has happened.

---

The part she circles back to, more than once, is Jacksonville State.

The job there was supposed to be the thing. Teaching show choir. Working with students who wanted to be there. Changing the way the program felt.

She did change it. Jerseys instead of uniforms. Converse instead of dress shoes. Harder music. “Hamilton,” she says. “We killed it.”

Students responded. They showed up. They cried when she left.

But the structure around it didn’t hold.

A mentor who didn’t mentor. Complaints about the difficulty of the music. A contract that wasn’t renewed. Official reasons that don’t quite match what it felt like on the ground.

Five hundred dollars a month, for four days a week. Rehearsals. Performances. Extra work that doesn’t get named as extra.

“It was a dream job,” she says. “Just wrong timing.”

She doesn’t let it go cleanly.

There are other stories inside that time. Things said about how she looked. Roles she wasn’t supposed to have, even when she could sing them better than anyone else. Students whispering during her solos.

“I wish I could have told people what I was going through,” she says.

At forty-one, she’s trying to name it differently.

“I’m not going to minimize myself.”

---

“Mom to Go is who I am,” she says.

It’s tied to the same thing that pushed her into entrepreneurship in the first place.

Her boys play travel soccer. It costs money. It takes time. It requires someone to be there.

“I want to be with them,” she says.

So she builds work that moves with them.

What started as cooking for people shifts into teaching. Weekly classes. Grocery lists sent ahead of time.

“It doesn’t have to be hard,” she says. “It just has to be planned.”

She still caters. Still does events. Still makes the jerk chicken people ask for by name.

But the larger idea is bigger than any one dish.

She talks about Martha Stewart. Ina Garten. Retail shelves. Aprons, tableware, cutlery. A full line. All of it hot pink.

“I want it everywhere,” she says.

Then, more directly:

“I want to be a millionaire, Matt.”

She doesn’t soften it. Doesn’t laugh it off after.

It sits there as a statement of intent.

---

She still talks about the people who hurt her in college.

She also runs a business that didn’t exist before everything fell apart.

Both are true.

She takes her medication. Pays attention to routines. Sunlight. Movement.

“We’re doing the best we can,” she says.

Then she goes back to work.

Building something her kids could take and run with if they wanted to.

Something that lasts.
